The Space Between — Script Review

By Ari Goren • Reviewed April 25, 2026 • 13-page short film draft
Summary. The story works. The emotional spine is clear, the central image (the stone, the reflection in the glass) is strong, and the restraint of the final scene lands. Below is a categorized punch list of fixable issues — typos through structure.

What's Working

  • The opening breakfast / inhaler / pinky promise exchange establishes Sarah's character entirely through small instructions — show, don't tell.
  • The window-reflection beat on pages 5–6, where Sarah and James "occupy the same space in the glass," is a strong central image.
  • The tunnel echo on page 9 — the voiceover only audible in darkness — is a beautiful restraint choice.
  • The two pockets / two hands ending gives the audience the metaphor without telegraphing it.

Grammar & Punctuation

  • p.2 — "But what if its really early?" → it's (same fix twice on this page)
  • p.4 — I love you too, Mommy. (add comma of direct address; capitalize Mommy)
  • p.13 — last action line is missing a period: "…like he's still deciding what it means"

Capitalization

  • p.2 — JAMES's dialogue: "even on Saturdays?" → "Even on Saturdays?" (dialogue lines start uppercase)
  • p.3, p.4 — beat. appears lowercase, but it's Beat. everywhere else. Pick one (industry standard is Beat.)
  • p.4 — mommy as direct address → Mommy
  • p.11 — "offering the stone to dan" → Dan
  • p.11 — "Something flicks In his mind." → in

Continuity Error

Eyes opening twice. End of p.9: "James opens his eyes." Then p.11 opens with: "James opens his eyes, looks down at the stone." — his eyes were already open from p.10's "What is that?" exchange.

Fix: delete the second "opens his eyes," or change p.9 to keep his eyes closed through Dan's question.

Format / Screenplay Convention

p.13 — broken V.O. block. As laid out, the dialogue is split awkwardly:

JAMES (V.O.) I'll call Mommy tonight. Tell her all about the train ride. Beat.

The orphan line and trailing Beat. should be:

JAMES (V.O.) I'll call Mommy tonight. Tell her all about the train ride. Beat.
  • p.7 — The spreadsheet description is a dense paragraph of action. Cleaner: lead with INSERT - LAPTOP SCREEN and break it into 2–3 short visual lines so the reader knows they're meant to read the screen.
  • p.5 — Three INT. TRAIN - CONTINUOUS headers stack within seconds. Some directors love the rhythm; a script reader may flag it. Could collapse two into one.
  • p.12 — mid type → mid-type (or mid-typing)

Verify With the Writer (Not Errors)

  • Surname mismatch. James and Dan are SHNIDER; Sarah is ORCHIN. Intentional (kept maiden name / already separated)? If yes, fine. If no, align them.
  • "SHNIDER" spelling. Unusual vs. Schneider / Snider — stylized on purpose?
  • Dan's Bloomington/limestone speech (p.4). Dan says "the ground they're standing on" while they're 200 miles away in Kentucky. Reads slightly off geographically. Probably intentional (nervous trivia-dumping) — but a director may push back.
  • Buried backstory. Lines like "a man who knows exactly what he's done" (p.3), "older than this argument" (p.4), the phone photo (p.9), and the undealt business cards (p.13) all hint at something specific — affair, forced career move, betrayal. The ambiguity is a craft choice. If the writer wants the audience to land on a specific read, one or two of these may need to point harder.
