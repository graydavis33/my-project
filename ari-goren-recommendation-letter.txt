What stood out to me the first time I read "The Space Between" is that it reads like a film, not just a script. A lot of young writers fill the page with dialogue and exposition. Ari does the opposite — he trusts the image. The moment on the train where Sarah and James's reflections briefly share the same frame in the window, and then slip out of alignment as the train pulls away, is the kind of single visual idea that can carry an entire scene. As someone who works behind the camera, that's the language I'm looking for in a script.

Ari also uses the environment itself to do emotional work. Early in the train ride, he writes: "The landscape rolls by outside. Curving hills. Fields. Small towns that blur together." Pages later, as the train nears Bloomington: "The landscape has changed. Less rolling hills. Flatter. More industrial. Warehouses. Power lines." Same train, same boy pressed against the same window — but the world outside has gone hard. That's the kind of paired image system a writer puts on the page so a director can plan an entire visual arc around it. Most scripts don't think that far ahead.

Ari's writing is restrained in a way that takes real confidence. He doesn't explain what the audience can already read on a face. James's quiet question — "Is it because of what I said this morning?" — lands harder than any expository line could, because by that point we already understand everything the parents aren't saying to each other. The same is true of the gummy bears, the unsealed stack of business cards in Dan's pocket, and the stone James carries through the entire trip. Small physical objects holding the full emotional weight of the story. Those are the details a director shoots with real care.

The script is also intelligently producible. A small cast, contained locations, and dialogue-light scenes mean an indie-level budget can support a high-quality result. Almost every emotional beat is achievable in-camera — the tunnel voiceover, for example, costs nothing to execute but delivers one of the most affecting moments in the script. That's the sign of a filmmaker who has already thought about the craft beyond just the page.

What ultimately convinces me of "The Space Between" is its emotional honesty. It approaches separation through a six-year-old's eyes without slipping into sentimentality, and it gives the father room to be flawed and silent without becoming a villain. Audiences who have lived through any version of a family coming apart will recognize themselves in this story, and the festival circuit consistently rewards exactly this kind of intimate, character-led storytelling.

I have full confidence in Ari Goren as a writer, and as a filmmaker capable of bringing this script to the screen. "The Space Between" deserves the support it needs to be made.
