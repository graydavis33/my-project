# The ONLY 5 Lines You Need To Tell Any Story
**Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NeTJRCycYXQ · **Channel:** Tim Runia (filmmaker, Netherlands) · **Length:** 9:31 · **Format:** Story-driven talking-head + B-roll (documentary-flavored)

## Why this video matters for our playbook
Every other reference in this folder (01–08) is about **hooks, retention, and sentence-craft** — the tactics that keep someone watching once you have a story. This one is upstream of all of them: it's about **finding the story in the first place.** It's the only reference in the set built by a *documentary/story-driven* filmmaker rather than a hook-tactics creator, which makes it the missing front-half of Gray's long-form process. Where Kallaway's 01-killer-script tells you how to *open* a video, Runia tells you how to know what the video *is* before you write a word. This is the antidote to Gray's exact documented pain (from the 2026-06-14 production review): long-form that's "a tutorial, not a story." The Five-Line Method is the cheapest possible tool for locking the emotional core *before* filming the A-roll — which is precisely what the long-form SOP now demands.

## The hook (first 30 seconds, word-for-word)
> I used to spend hours trying to plan a video and still have no idea what I was actually trying to say. I would make an outline, rewrite, scrap it and start all over again. Or I'd skip it all and just start filming, hoping I would figure it all out in the edit. But then I'd be staring at a messy timeline with no clue what the story was actually supposed to be. I was overthinking, totally getting lost in the details. Until I started doing something so stupidly simple, I couldn't believe it actually worked.

**Why it works:**
- **Relatable pain cold open** — names the viewer's exact frustration (messy timeline, no clear story) before pitching anything. Pure Pain-Lead (see 04-irresistible-hooks).
- **Open loop via withheld mechanism** — "something so stupidly simple" promises a payload without naming it. You have to keep watching to learn what the five lines are.
- **Credibility through specificity** — the overthinking spiral is so precisely described that the viewer trusts he's lived it.
- **Contrarian undertone** — "simple" is positioned against the viewer's instinct that good story needs complexity.

## The framework: The Five-Line Story Method
Write **five sentences** before you do anything else. Each answers one question. Together they ARE the story — the "emotional core" / "life thread" you protect through every later decision.

| # | Line | The question it answers |
|---|------|------------------------|
| 1 | **Situation** | Where are we, what's happening, what does the world look like? (the starting point) |
| 2 | **Desire** | What does the character want — to obtain or to reach? |
| 3 | **Conflict** | What gets in the way? Where's the tension / the obstacle? |
| 4 | **Change** | What shifts? The turning point — big or small, an insight or an action. |
| 5 | **Result** | How does it end? What's the new reality, and how is it different from line 1? |

**Why each line earns its place (his teaching):**
- **Situation** gives the viewer a clear starting point — who's in the story and what world we're in.
- **Desire** sparks curiosity and *investment* — the audience starts caring whether the goal is reached, and often the character's want mirrors their own.
- **Conflict** is non-negotiable: "if there's no tension, no obstacle, no challenge, there's not really a reason to keep watching." (This is the same engine as the But/Therefore rule in 02-master-storyteller.)
- **Change** marks the turning point — shows growth, gives "the feeling of movement forward."
- **Result** delivers closure: a satisfying takeaway that ties the whole thing together and shows the before→after delta.

## The two ideas that make this more than a worksheet

**1. The Launchpad principle (the most important takeaway).**
> "These five lines aren't your whole video. They are some sort of launchpad."

The five lines are not the script. They are (a) the thing you write *first* to find the core, and (b) the thing you *return to* whenever the project overwhelms you. His exact test: when you're drowning in choices, look back at your five lines and ask **"can I connect what I'm building back to one of these five lines?"** If a clip, a transition, a VO can't be tied to a line, it's noise — cut it. This is a decision-filter for the entire edit, not just a planning step.

**2. Expansion: 5 → 10 → 20 → whole video.**
The same core scales. He literally demos his own 8-minute YouTube video collapsed to 5 lines, then re-expanded to 10 lines (same skeleton, more richness), and says he could do 20, and 20 → the whole script. **Going from 5 to 10 lines doesn't change the core — it only adds richness and detail.** So you draft the 5, pressure-test that the arc holds, then expand only along lines that already exist. You never add a beat that isn't anchored to one of the five.

## Worked example — The Lion King (his demo)
| Line | The Lion King |
|------|---------------|
| Situation | Simba is a young prince, excited to one day rule the Pride Lands. |
| Desire | He wants to prove he's brave and worthy of being king. |
| Conflict | After his father's death, Simba runs away — ashamed, afraid to face his past. |
| Change | He grows up; with help from friends and a vision of his father, he returns to confront Scar. |
| Result | He restores balance to the Pride Lands and becomes who he was meant to be. |

## Worked example — his own first-YouTube-video (5-line → 10-line)
**5-line version:** Filmmaker from the Netherlands, loved making videos since a kid → dreamed of being a YouTuber telling stories from the heart, not just for clients → but self-doubt and overthinking pulled him back every time → letting go of the "YouTube dream" made him realize he just wanted to *create stories*, so he sat down and started → and that first step (this very video) pushed him to go all in.

**10-line version:** same skeleton, but the conflict expands into the specific fears ("you can't go from making ads for big companies to YouTube," "too late, too old, not enough skills/gear/security"), and the change expands into the internal reframe (stripping away subscribers/money to find the joy of creating). **Identical core, more texture.**

## How Gray should steal this
1. **Use it as Step 0 of every long-form, before the cold-open hook.** Our documentary arc in `playbook.md` starts at the A-roll cold open. This sits *in front of that*: write the five lines for the week/subject first, find the core, THEN write the heavily-edited 30-second hook from line 1 + line 3 (situation + the conflict/turn). The hook frameworks in 01/04 take the five lines as input.
2. **The Story Lens (02-master-storyteller) IS line 2 + line 3.** "Desire vs. Conflict" is the prism. Declaring your lens out loud early = telling the audience the desire and the obstacle in the first minute.
3. **Make it the edit's cut-filter.** Tape the five lines above the timeline. Any clip that can't connect to a line gets cut. This is the practical fix for doc-as-vlog sprawl.
4. **Scales down to shorts too.** A 30–60s Sai short is just a tight 5-line: situation (his old way) → desire (what he wanted) → conflict (what broke) → change (the shift) → result (the lesson/new reality). Most of Sai's strongest voice-memo shorts already follow this without naming it.
5. **Pairs with But/Therefore (02) and Expectations-vs-Reality (01).** Five lines = the skeleton; But/Therefore = the connective tissue between expanded beats; Expectations-vs-Reality = making sure the Result *beats* what line 1 set up.

## One-line summary
Before any hook, gear, or edit: write **Situation → Desire → Conflict → Change → Result.** That's the emotional core. Everything you build must trace back to one of those five lines — and the same five lines expand, unchanged at the core, all the way up to the finished video.
