# How to Write a Killer Script in 2026
**Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PJWWGq_Yuoo · **Channel:** Personal Brand Launch · **Length:** 7:00 · **Views:** 10K · **Format:** Talking-head

## Why this video matters for our playbook
At 7 minutes, this is the shortest video in the batch and the most utility-dense — there is almost zero filler, and the entire video is one tight teach-tactic-evidence-proof loop. Unlike Kallaway's effects-heavy, edit-driven style, Personal Brand Launch leans on a calm, conversational talking-head delivery and uses external clip montages as her "B-roll engine" — every claim is immediately demonstrated with 3-5 stacked example reels. This is the cleanest model in the batch for how a single creator can teach a framework in under 7 minutes without losing retention.

## The hook (first 30 seconds, word-for-word)
> I grew my Instagram account from this to this not once but twice along with multiple different client accounts in multiple different niches. Isn't that right, Bearsy? Guys, I know how to grow on Instagram with short form content and I have been doing it for years. In this video, I'm going to show you how to create killer scripts that get meaningful results like this, this, and this. Today, we're going to cover step by step how you can get the same results with a strategy I bet you never heard of before. Let's go.

**Why it works:**
- **Receipts-first credibility stack:** opens with a visual proof claim ("from this to this") before any teaching — establishes she has earned the right to teach this. Doubles down with "not once but twice" and "multiple client accounts in multiple niches" to preempt "lucky/one-niche" objections.
- **Pet pattern interrupt ("Isn't that right, Bearsy?"):** a tiny humanizing beat that breaks the credentials monologue and signals warmth/personality — keeps the open feeling like a friend, not a guru pitch.
- **Curiosity gap + promise:** "a strategy I bet you never heard of before" plants an explicit information gap, framed against the viewer's existing knowledge. The triple "like this, this, and this" [visual not captured — likely three reel thumbnails] is a tactile preview of the payoff.

## Key teaching points / frameworks (extracted verbatim where possible)
1. **Script structures are fill-in-the-blank templates for the entire script.** "A script structure is basically just a fill-in-the-blank template for your entire script." Examples cited: "If you drink apple cider vinegar with lemon…", "If you take creatine…", "If you add cinnamon and cayenne pepper…"
2. **Four script-structure categories:**
   - **Easy** ("If you do X, then Y" / numbered repetition / color-name montage)
   - **Complex** ("Did you know if you…" hyper-educational, hard to spot the pattern)
   - **Educational** ("Three levels of cookies / tomato pasta / mountains / skincare")
   - **Storytelling** ("Is it possible to [outcome] in [timeframe] even though [obstacle]? Yes.")
3. **The 5x Outlier Rule.** "Videos from small to mid-size creators that performed way better than their average video… that video got 5x the amount of views and that creator is following." This is her single filter for "is this template worth stealing?"
4. **The 4-Step Steal-and-Templatize Workflow:**
   1. Find outlier video (passes 5x rule).
   2. Three dots → Copy link → paste into **gettranscribe.ai** → Get transcript.
   3. ChatGPT prompt verbatim: *"This is a transcript from a viral video. Please make it into a script template that can be used for any niche. Keep the overall format/structure of the video and just make it the fill-in-the-blank version."* Then paste transcript.
   4. Save to Google Doc, build a "bank" of templates.
5. **Easy vs. Complex tradeoff (with personal receipts).** Easy structures = high views, poor view-to-follower ratio (her rating reel: 1M views, 200 followers). Complex structures = fewer views but dramatically better follower conversion. Her recommendation: "Lean towards more complex script structures right now."
6. **The "trends are just script structures" reframe.** "All trends are actually just repeatable script structures. And this is exactly how they're created, whether people really understand that or not."

## Retention tactics observed
- **Pattern interrupts (script-level):** "Isn't that right, Bearsy?" (pet break), "Sorry, just had to get that little PSA" (acknowledges the tangent), and the constant cut to external example reels every 20-40 seconds.
- **Open loops opened and closed:** Opens with "a strategy I bet you never heard of before" (closed at 1:50 when she names "script structures"). Opens a secondary loop ("you still need viral hooks") and intentionally leaves it open, pointing to her other video — converts retention into channel watch-time.
- **Curiosity gaps:** "Did you notice anything similar about these two videos?" forces the viewer to actively look rather than passively listen. "Most people wouldn't recognize was actually a great structure" frames complex structures as a hidden edge.
- **Transitions between sections (verbatim):**
  - "Well, believe it or not, there are also script structures that go viral in any niche."
  - "Once you understand this, you will learn all trends are actually just repeatable script structures."
  - "Sorry, just had to get that little PSA. Okay."
  - "So now that you understand what script structures are, how to find good script structures, and how to make script structures, now you need to understand the difference between low-effort content and high effort content."
- **How does she keep a tight 7-min video tight?** Three mechanisms: (1) every claim is followed within 5 seconds by 3-5 stacked example clips — the example reels do the visual variety job that B-roll would do in a longer video; (2) she uses a strict "name concept → show 3 examples → next concept" beat structure with no doubling back; (3) the actionable workflow is a numbered 4-step list — viewer knows exactly when the payoff arrives, which discourages drop-off. Side-channel retention: she explicitly hands off to her hook-structures video, turning the end of this video into the start of the next.

## CTA / ending structure
Ending is soft and channel-oriented rather than offer-oriented. The final 60 seconds walk through the receipts story ("I found this one video online… it has honestly done extraordinary in terms of views, engagement, leads, followers"), reinforces the recommendation (lean complex), reframes the strategy as obvious-but-rare ("so few people actually do this"), then closes with "Happy scripting and follow for more social media marketing education." No lead magnet, no link in bio mention, no urgency. The earlier mid-video CTA ("just watch this video — I'll teach you exactly how") is the harder ask — she uses the end of this video to launch the viewer into her hook-structures video.

## Apply to Gray's content
- **Documentary use:** The 4-step numbered framework with verbatim prompts is the gold standard for "give the viewer a workflow." For a long-form doc on creator workflow, build the same skeleton: name concept → show evidence montage → categorize subtypes → give one measurable filter (her 5x rule) → walk through the literal step-by-step → end with a personal-receipts story. Specifically for Gray: the "PSA" mid-video tangent ("Sorry, just had to get that little PSA") is a great structural move — gives him permission to drop in a values beat without breaking the arc.
- **Talking-head use:** This is the closest video in the batch to Gray's likely sit-down format. Steal: (1) open with stacked receipts before any teaching, (2) ask a diagnostic question at ~30s that makes the viewer actively look at examples, (3) use external clip montages as the primary B-roll engine — Gray doesn't need fancy motion graphics if he can pull 3-5 outlier reels per claim, (4) end with a soft follow CTA, not a hard pitch.
- **Shorts use:** Her own taxonomy IS the short. Gray can take any single beat — e.g., the 5x outlier rule, or the 4-step templatize workflow — and turn it into a 45-60s short. Her "Is it possible to [outcome] in [timeframe] even though [obstacle]? Yes." template is directly transplantable to videography ("Is it possible to color-grade a wedding video in 20 minutes even though you've never used DaVinci? Yes."). The "Did you know if you…" structure also maps cleanly to editor-tips shorts.

## Style notes (comparison to Kallaway)
**Pacing:** Personal Brand Launch is markedly slower and calmer per word, but compresses more concepts per minute because she uses example montages instead of monologuing. Kallaway tends to front-load energy and use cuts/zooms for retention; Personal Brand Launch lets the external clips do the visual variety work.
**Structure:** Both use receipts-first hooks, but Personal Brand Launch's arc is more pedagogical — concept → taxonomy → filter → workflow → tradeoff — while Kallaway is more narrative/persuasive. Her video is essentially a numbered framework wrapped in a story; Kallaway's tend to be stories wrapped in a thesis.
**Tone:** Conversational, warm, occasionally self-interrupting ("Isn't that right, Bearsy?", "Sorry, just had to get that little PSA"). Kallaway is more declarative and high-confidence; Personal Brand Launch reads as "smart friend with a system."
**What each does better:** Kallaway is better at sticky one-liners and high-energy hooks that work as standalone shorts. Personal Brand Launch is better at delivering a complete, actionable workflow in minimum time — her video is more *useful* per minute, with verbatim tools (gettranscribe.ai), verbatim prompts (the ChatGPT prompt), and a verbatim filter (5x rule). For Gray's documentary work, Kallaway is the model for hook craft; Personal Brand Launch is the model for teaching density and short-format discipline.
