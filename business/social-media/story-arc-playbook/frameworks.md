# Frameworks Library

Every named framework pulled from the 8 reference videos in this playbook, organized by what you're trying to do (hook, structure a story, hold attention, find ideas, write the script). Treat this as a lookup index — when you're stuck, find the section that matches your problem.

## How to use this

**Pick by job-to-be-done, not by source video.**
1. Writing a hook? → Hook frameworks section.
2. Outlining the body of a video? → Story / arc frameworks.
3. Losing viewers in the middle? → Retention / engagement frameworks.
4. Don't know what to make a video about? → Discovery / research frameworks.
5. Sitting in front of a blank page? → Production / scripting frameworks.

**Rules of thumb:**
- Always run a draft through the **Hook Stack** (verbal + written + visual, all working inside 3 seconds) and the **4 Hook Commandments** before recording.
- For long-form, plan **re-hooks** at every 2-3 minutes. For shorts, plan **one re-hook around the 20-25 second mark**.
- For shorts specifically, the **3-second triple-hook standard** and the **Shock Score test** are non-negotiable.
- "Jargon" — when this doc says "open loop" it means asking a question or teasing something so the viewer keeps watching to find out the answer. When it says "pattern interrupt" it means a sudden change (joke, clip, visual, tone shift) that jolts a bored brain awake.

## Hook frameworks

### Kallaway 3-Step Hook Formula (from 04-irresistible-hooks)
**The single most stealable hook formula in the whole library. This is your default.**

**Definition:** A 3-line opener that earns the right to keep talking by stopping the scroll, making the viewer lean in, then snapping them sideways with something they didn't expect.

**Formula:**
1. **Context Lean** — first 1-2 lines do two things at once: (a) state the topic clearly so the right viewer self-selects in, (b) make them lean in via one of four levers: common ground, benefit/pain, metaphor, or mind-blowing fact.
2. **Scroll Stop Interjection** — a single setup line (NOT the payoff) that uses a contrast word: *but, however, yet, although, therefore, on the other hand*. Goal: "I need you to start leaning in and then immediately I need you to stop being able to move forward like you ran into a window."
3. **Contrarian Snapback** — a sentence that goes the opposite direction of the initial lean, still on topic. "The bigger the shock, the bigger the snap, the better the effect."

**Verbatim examples:**
- "The tech in the Vegas sphere is insane — biggest screen ever built, 20 times bigger than an IMAX. But get this: the screen is actually the least impressive part of the whole thing. Because the most impressive part is the audio. This is going to blow your mind." (Sphere video, 8M views)
- "There are three massive mistakes people are making with their mortgage. The average person pays an extra $122,000 per year. Now most people think it's because of high interest rates, but it turns out it has nothing to do with that. Because the biggest waste of money is actually coming from..." (B2B mortgage example)

**Universal phrasing wrapper:** *"If you want to achieve X, don't do Y, do Z."*

**When to use:** Every video. This is the default. Especially gold for shorts (compresses to 5-7 seconds).

### Kallaway 5-Part Intro Hook (from 01-killer-script)
**Use this for long-form (talking-head and documentary intros) where you have 15-30 seconds to build trust.**

**Definition:** A 5-part opener for longer videos that confirms the click, names the conventional wisdom, flips it, drops proof, and previews the plan.

**Formula:**
1. **Immediate context (click confirmation)** — first line confirms the title's promise. If your title is "How to write killer scripts," your first line should literally say "today we're talking about writing killer scripts."
2. **Common belief** — restate what most people walk in believing ("this is an art").
3. **Contrarian take** — flip it ("they all run the exact same script writing process").
4. **Proof** — credibility burst ("billion views, a million followers").
5. **Plan** — name the structure of what's coming ("proven five-step framework").

Parts 4 and 5 can swap order.

**Verbatim example:** "Today we're talking about writing killer scripts. The reason winning creators go viral so often is because of their script writing... This is an art. I've analyzed hundreds of the top creators across every niche and they all run the exact same script writing process. So in this video I'm going to break it all down. This is the proven five-step framework for how to write killer scripts that keep viewers hooked. By the way, I'm Kallaway, I've done over a billion views, I have a million followers..."

**When to use:** Long-form sit-down talking heads (Sharran/Hormozi style) and documentary intros where you have runway. Don't use for shorts — too long.

### Kallaway 9 Hook Formats (from 05-10x-faster)
**Use this as a menu when the 3-Step formula doesn't fit naturally. Pick one based on your topic.**

**Definition:** Nine proven hook templates Kallaway pulls from analyzing thousands of viral videos. Don't invent — pick one and remix.

1. **Secret Reveal** — unknown insight / future implication. *"Google just released four free AI trainings that make it way faster to learn AI."*
2. **Case Study** — subject achieved a result in an unexpected way. *"Alex Hormosi just sold $100 million worth of books in 3 days using this five-step method."*
3. **Comparison** — A vs B contrast. *"What's the difference between a beginner creator versus an experienced one?"*
4. **Question** — implant curiosity directly. *"Can you bake sourdough bread in under an hour using only household ingredients?"*
5. **Education** — step-by-step process. *"Here's the easiest way to use ChatGPT to create content."*
6. **List** — ordered set. *"These are the five best Premiere Pro editing templates you can use for free."*
7. **Contrarian** — bold take in the first sentence. *"Jaguar's rebrand is one of the worst design blunders in the history of the world."*
8. **Personal Experience** — first-person story setup. *"When I was in college, I used this sleep technique to only have to sleep 3 hours per night."*
9. **Problem** — agitate the pain to set up the solution. *"If you struggle with acne, this is one obvious solution that will clear it right up."*

**When to use:** When picking the angle for a specific video. Study your niche, see which formats are working there, pick that one. Don't pick the contrarian format if no contrarian content is winning in your space.

### Kallaway 4 Hook Commandments (from 05-10x-faster)
**The quality filter. Every hook you write must pass all four.**

**Definition:** Four non-negotiable criteria. Run every hook draft through this checklist before recording.

1. **Alignment** — "Hooks are actually three parts: visual hook, spoken hook, and text hook. All three must align." (Same idea as the PBL Hook Stack.)
2. **Speed to Value** — zero delay, zero fluff. The first hit of value lands inside the hook itself, or immediately after.
3. **Clarity** — "If the viewer only hears the first sentence and you stop the video there, is it clear what's coming next or is it up in the air?" If a stranger can't predict the topic from sentence one, rewrite.
4. **Curiosity** — must open a question loop in the viewer's mind that demands the next line to close.

**When to use:** Every single hook. This is the final gate before you press record.

### PBL / Ava 7 Hook Angles (from 08-irresistible-hooks)
**Operator-level companion to the Kallaway 9 — overlaps but is more directly tied to script structure. Use for the niche of business / how-to content Gray makes.**

**Definition:** Seven angles you can take on the same topic. One topic × 7 angles = 7 different videos.

1. **Tutorial angle** — step-by-step process, framework, or acronym.
2. **Comparison angle** — compare two actions/methods for the same result, show which is better.
3. **Myth-bust / common mistake angle** — debunk a belief in your niche.
4. **Do's / Don'ts angle** — right way vs. wrong way.
5. **Educational tip / hack angle** — teach a niche lesson, tip, or hack.
6. **Transformation angle** — niche before-and-after result.
7. **Challenge angle** — create or participate in a niche-related challenge.

**Multiplier:** Each angle can be delivered as **educational** OR **storytelling** mode → 7 × 2 = 14 distinct videos per topic. This is the direct antidote to Gray's "repetitive content takes too long" pain point.

**Verbatim cross-niche templates to remix:**
- *"Is it possible to [outcome] in [timeframe] even though [obstacle]?"*
- *"[X] days/years ago..."* (*"600 days ago..."*, *"6 years ago..."*)
- *"Did you know if you..."*
- *"Three levels of [thing]..."* (*"Three levels of tomato pasta"*, *"Three levels of cookies"*)
- *"Smart [person] vs. dumb [person] when it comes to [activity]..."*
- *"This is a picture of my first [thing] ever..."*

**Applied to Gray's niche (videography/editing):**
- *"Three levels of color grading"*
- *"Smart editor vs. dumb editor when it comes to transitions"*
- *"Is it possible to edit a wedding video in 20 minutes even though you've never used DaVinci?"*

**When to use:** When you have a topic and need to spin it into a content batch. Run every topic through all 7 angles before deciding which 1-3 to film.

### PBL Hook Stack (from 07-killer-short and 08-irresistible-hooks)
**The 3-in-3 rule. Non-negotiable for shorts.**

**Definition:** Every short needs three hooks landing simultaneously inside the first 3 seconds: verbal, written, visual.

**Formula:**
- **Verbal hook** — what they HEAR in the first 3 seconds.
- **Written hook** — what they READ on-screen in the first 3 seconds (the bold 3-5 word title overlay).
- **Visual hook** — what they SEE in the first 3 seconds (the action, prop, or scene composition).

**Rule:** "The more hooks you use in your video, the bigger the chance of winning."

**Verbatim hook-stack example (styling niche):**
- Written: *"Hairstyle neckline guide"*
- Verbal: *"This is the best hairstyle for different tops"*
- Visual: a clone effect (two of her on-screen at once)

**When to use:** Every short. Before you record, you write all three on paper. If you can only fill in two, the short isn't ready.

### Visual Hook (Kallaway version — from 02-master-storyteller and 04-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** A visual element on screen at the very first frame, before any words land.

**Rules:**
- "Visual hooks are 10 times more effective than audio-only hooks... people's eyes perceive faster than their ears can hear."
- 3-5 words on screen in a big bold font that reinforces the context lean.
- **Goldilocks motion principle:** "Too much motion and you're going to overwhelm them. Too little motion and you're going to bore them. You need just enough motion." Subtle motion examples: leaning forward/back, walking forward, holding gear, adjusting camera.
- **Pre-empt unfamiliar terms** — if your concept has a weird name, show the more familiar version FIRST on screen, then the new term. (Kallaway used "Future of Home Design" before "Life-Size Floor Plans.")

**Verbatim example:** Kevin from Epic Gardening showing a vivid red strawberry on screen before saying a single word. Colin & Samir's Ray-Ban Meta clip (60M views) opened with the product visible immediately.

**When to use:** Every video, especially shorts. Frame 1 = something visually arresting.

### Cult Hopping (from 04-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** Wrap an unknown idea in a known reference (celebrity, brand, movement, cultural moment) to give the viewer comfort scaffolding.

**Mechanism:** "When people hear something that they've heard before, it unlocks a comfort in their subconscious. The opposite is also true — too much unknown = you start to feel dumb."

**Verbatim example:** A tax-planning video referencing "Taylor Swift's financial advisor planning her estate around the Eras Tour earnings."

**When to use:** When you're still small/growing and need to "draft off the credibility" of something popular. For Gray: "MrBeast's editor uses this one trick," "This is how A24 grades skin tones."

---

### Benefit / Pain Lead (from 04-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** Lead the hook with the outcome the viewer wants (or the pain they want to escape), not with the mechanism.

**Weak version:** "You should be taking magnesium because it's one of 21 core building block minerals."
**Strong version:** *"If you want better sleep, you need to be taking magnesium."*

**Mechanism:** "Even if someone is against supplements or against magnesium, they will likely wait and hear you out because their desire to solve the pain of not sleeping well is so strong."

**Applied to Gray:** *"If you want clients to actually book you..."* / *"If you want your wedding edits to feel cinematic..."*

**When to use:** Default opening pattern for benefit-driven content (most tutorials and tips).

---

### Curiosity Loop principle (from 04-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** "If you want to tell a great story, you need the viewer to watch the first line and feel so compelled that they can't dream of doing anything else than watching the second line and the third line and so on. The domino effect... the deeper the loop the more curious you are, the more I've hooked you."

**When to use:** This is the underlying mental model behind every hook framework. If a line of your hook doesn't make the viewer want the next line, cut it.

---

### Highway Metaphor (from 04-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** The mental model for what a hook has to physically accomplish.

"You are driving down the highway at 70 mph. I need you to see something, stop, turn around, and come back the other way." Three jobs: **stop the car → exit the highway → turn around.**

**When to use:** Sanity-check any hook against this. If it doesn't make the scroller stop AND turn back, it's not a hook.

## Story / arc frameworks

### Hook → Body → Re-hook → Body → Outro (the master architecture, from 03-cant-stop-watching)
**This is the structural skeleton for every video Gray makes. Memorize the shape.**

**Definition:** The 5-beat architecture that every winning long-form and short-form video follows.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook** — establish context + aggravate pain point OR tease benefit in the first 2-3 seconds.
2. **First Body** — first major teaching point or story beat.
3. **Re-hook** — "the re-aggravation of the pain point originally triggered in the hook by showing that there's a better solution still out there. Hey, what I said is good, but what's coming next is great." Timing rules: **20-25 seconds in for shorts, 2-3 minutes in for YouTube long-form.**
4. **Second Body** — next major teaching point, raised stakes.
5. **Outro** — summarize value OR extend with a lead magnet / native embed.

**When to use:** Every video. Build the outline against this shape before you write a single line of script.

---

### Kallaway "The Dance" (Context ↔ Conflict, from 02-master-storyteller)
**Definition:** "All great stories are like a dance between context and conflict. You give a little context — the characters on a mission, they're doing something — and then, oh oh no, here comes a conflict."

**Mechanism:** "Conflicts create open loops in the brain and then context helps close those loops."

**When to use:** Especially for documentary editing. When sequencing raw footage, audit every transition: is this a *context beat* or a *conflict beat*? If it's neither, cut it.

---

### But / Therefore rule (Trey Parker / Matt Stone, from 02-master-storyteller)
**The single best editing test in the library. Use it on every cut.**

**Definition:** Between any two beats in your story, the connector should be either "but" or "therefore" — never "and then."

**Verbatim source (Matt Stone / Trey Parker NYU talk):** "If the words 'and then' belong between those beats, you got something pretty boring. What should happen between every beat is either the word 'therefore' or 'but.'"

**Verbatim proof:** Kallaway's own Stanley Cup video — "four but-then conflict loops in the first 30 seconds."

**Diagnostic:** Read your outline out loud. Insert "and then" between every bullet. If it makes sense, that's a problem — rewrite the transitions to be causal (*therefore*) or contrastive (*but*).

**When to use:** Outline review (long-form), cut review (documentary), every sentence-to-sentence transition (shorts).

### Kallaway 7 Story Structures (from 05-10x-faster)
**Pick one as the skeleton for your video. Each one pairs naturally with certain hook formats.**

1. **Breakdown** — hook → initial context shock → what/how → why it matters → outro. Pair with: secret reveal, question, comparison, contrarian.
2. **Newscaster** — hook → full context → optional short take → outro. Pair with: secret reveal, question, comparison, contrarian, problem.
3. **Case Study Explainer** — hook → framework (2-4 pieces) → outro. Pair with: case study, question, comparison.
4. **Listicle** — hook → list items → outro. Pair with: list, problem.
5. **Problem Solver** — hook → problem (+ agitation) → solution → outro. Pair with: problem, question, contrarian.
6. **Tutorial** — hook → step-by-step (contingent steps) → outro. Pair with: education hook primarily.
7. **Educational / Personal Story** — hook → personal experience with start/middle/end → outro. Pair with: personal experience, problem, contrarian.

**Tutorial vs Listicle vs Case Study (the disambiguation):**
- **Tutorial** = steps that go in succession leading to ONE outcome. Steps don't stand alone.
- **Listicle** = every unit can live on its own. They all point to the same outcome but they're independent.
- **Case Study** = less how-to, more analysis/reflection on the pieces that led to an outcome.

**When to use:** As soon as you've picked your hook, pick the story structure that pairs with it. Don't write a Tutorial body when your hook was a Contrarian.

### Story Flow + Atomic Messaging + Speed-to-Value (the 3 mistakes, from 03-cant-stop-watching)
**The three failure modes to avoid. Diagnostic framework for why a video isn't working.**

**Definition:** The three reasons most scripts fail.

1. **Story Flow Problem** — "when that line connecting the plot of the story is broken because you added things in that have nothing to do with the main premise. Extra details, disconnected tangents, broken plots, distractions." Storytelling is hypnosis; red herrings break the hypnosis.
2. **Comprehension Problem** — "If the viewer does not understand the words you're saying in the script, they're never going to get immersed in the story." Solved by **Atomic Messaging** (see Production section).
3. **Speed-to-Value Problem** — "Most people that make content are not getting to the value or the mention of value quick enough before the viewer bounces. Content is basically a race to see if you can signal value before they lose interest." Shorts timer: ~4 seconds. YouTube timer: 1-2 minutes.

**Fix for #1 — Line-by-line audit question:** *"Is this line a necessary piece of context or an intentional step in the logic flow I'm trying to make to get someone's understanding of the story to go up? Or is it a distraction and an unnecessary detail?"*

**When to use:** Every script draft gets audited against all three. If retention drops at a specific point, one of these three is the culprit — find which.

### PBL Script Taxonomy: Easy vs Complex vs Educational vs Storytelling (from 06-killer-script-2026)
**Definition:** Four categories of viral script structure, with a clear tradeoff between view count and follower conversion.

1. **Easy** — *"If you do X, then Y"* / numbered repetition / color-name montages. Examples: *"If you drink apple cider vinegar with lemon..."*, *"If you take creatine..."*
2. **Complex** — *"Did you know if you..."* hyper-educational, hard to spot the pattern. Looks like a normal piece of content but is built on a proven structure.
3. **Educational** — *"Three levels of [thing]..."* (cookies, tomato pasta, skincare).
4. **Storytelling** — *"Is it possible to [outcome] in [timeframe] even though [obstacle]? Yes."*

**Tradeoff (with personal receipts):**
- **Easy** = high views, poor view-to-follower ratio. PBL's rating reel: 1M views, 200 followers gained.
- **Complex** = fewer views, dramatically better follower conversion.

**Recommendation:** "Lean toward more complex script structures right now."

**When to use:** When deciding what flavor of short to make for the next batch. If Gray needs reach → Easy. If Gray needs followers/leads → Complex.

---

### Expectations vs Reality (the master mental model, from 01-killer-script)
**The single principle that governs every micro-decision in a script.**

**Definition:** "When reality beats expectations and things go better than expected, people are happy, they keep watching, and you win. But when expectations beat reality and things go worse than expected, people are not happy, they stop watching, and you lose."

**Public speaking analogy:** Low expectations + decent delivery = euphoria. High expectations + decent delivery = disappointment.

**Content implication:** Your title and thumbnail set expectations. Every line of your script either beats them or falls short. If you're just repeating base knowledge the viewer already has, you cannot beat their expectations.

**When to use:** Every editorial decision — title, thumbnail, hook, first body point, outro. Constantly ask: "did this beat the expectation set by the previous beat?"

## Retention / engagement frameworks

### Re-hook timing rules (from 03-cant-stop-watching)
**Definition:** A re-hook is "the re-aggravation of the pain point originally triggered in the hook by showing that there's a better solution still out there."

**Verbatim template:** *"Hey, what I said is good, but what's coming next is great."* / *"That point was super important, but if you don't also couple it with this one, then the magic is lost."*

**Timing:**
- **Shorts:** re-hook at the 20-25 second mark.
- **YouTube long-form:** re-hook every 2-3 minutes.

**When to use:** Always. Pre-plan re-hook lines at the outline stage, don't improvise them.

---

### The 6 Emotion Buckets + Emotional Transfer (from 05-10x-faster)
**Definition:** "The greater the transfer of emotion that your video has, A, the more the viewer will like it because the dopamine release, and B, the higher chance there is that they'll share it because they want to transfer that emotion to someone else."

**The 6 buckets:**
1. Awe / inspiration
2. Amusement / humor
3. Excitement / joy
4. Anger / outrage
5. Surprise / shock / curiosity
6. Sadness / empathy

**Tactic:** "Write the desired emotion at the top of my page as a reminder when I'm writing so that I can use that as a filter."

**When to use:** Before writing — pick the target emotion. After writing — re-read and ask "does this drive the emotion I picked?" If not, rewrite.

---

### Story Lenses (from 02-master-storyteller)
**Definition:** Your unique angle on a saturated topic. The differentiation move.

**Metaphor:** "Imagine a beam of light. As soon as you put a lens or a prism in front of the light, what people see will look differently."

**Verbatim proof:** Taylor Swift at the Super Bowl — most creators covered her outfit/arrival/reactions; Kallaway covered her **business impact on the NFL** → 1M views.

**Applied to Gray:** Don't cover "transitions in Premiere." Cover "transitions that wedding clients won't refund you for" or "transitions that signal you charge $10K instead of $1K."

**When to use:** Before you start writing — pre-decide the prism. Otherwise you'll make a video that already exists 50 times.

### Pattern interrupts (collected across all videos)
**Definition:** A sudden change in rhythm, tone, or modality that jolts a fading viewer back awake.

**Cataloged tactics:**
- Cross-domain analogies (Kallaway's music industry / album track 3 analogy mid-framework, the restaurant "free chocolate" analogy at outro).
- Self-deprecating jokes ("the easiest way to simplify is to actually be in sixth grade. That was a joke.")
- Strawman objections ("Listen, crazy guy waving his hands around. I'm a tax accountant...")
- Breaking the fourth wall ("I just did it again, that was another native embed.")
- Pet/family interruptions ("Isn't that right, Bearsy?")
- Confessional asides ("Sorry, I didn't have it in my notes to say that, but I really do want to emphasize that...")
- Off-script honesty ("Can I be transparent guys?")
- Cuts to external clips every 60-90 seconds (Kallaway always cuts away to a proof clip on that cadence).

**When to use:** At least one pattern interrupt every 60-90 seconds in long-form. In shorts, build at least one mid-clip cut or visual shift.

---

### Open loops + Curiosity gaps (collected across all videos)
**Definition:** Posing a question or teasing a reveal that the viewer's brain refuses to let go of until it gets the answer.

**Verbatim templates:**
- "Before we get into the framework, there is a critical first piece..."
- "This is deep deep deep psychology that you will not hear anywhere else on YouTube..."
- "The black magic that makes all this stuff really work..."
- "I left [the hook] for last because everybody always talks about the hook first."
- "I'm going to do a magic trick — because I used the same psychology formula on you for the hook in this video, and if you're still watching, that proves it worked."
- "What do these successful creators know that you don't?"

**Rules:**
- Open loops in the hook, pay them off later in the video — not all at once.
- Stack them: 3-4 nested open loops at different scales (one for the whole video, one for the current section, one for the current paragraph).

**When to use:** At outline stage — mark where each loop opens and where it closes.

---

### Zoom In / Zoom Out Rhythm (from 01-killer-script and 05-10x-faster)
**Definition:** Alternate big picture (theory, why it matters) with small picture (specific tactic, example) like a wave.

**Mechanism:** Adds comprehension. Lets viewers extract tactical pieces while staying locked into the larger story.

**Verbatim transitions to steal:**
- "Now let's zoom back out."
- "At this point in the process..."
- "So where are we..."
- "Let's pull back."

**When to use:** Every 3-5 minutes in long-form, give an orientation beat. In documentary, this is the editor's compass when assembling a 20+ minute cut.

---

### Storytelling-as-Hypnosis (from 03-cant-stop-watching)
**Definition:** "Great storytelling is like hypnosis. You want the viewer or listener to forget where they are and get fully immersed in the one single plot you're focused on... The more red herrings or unnecessary details you throw in, the more fragile and surface level that hypnosis becomes."

**When to use:** As the defensive frame for cutting brutally. Every B-roll moment, every aside, every "wait, also..." should be tested: does it deepen the hypnosis or break it?

---

### Gary Provost Rhythm Test (from 02-master-storyteller)
**Definition:** Vary sentence length to create musicality. "Don't just write words — write music."

**Diagnostic — the jagged edge test:** "If you look at my script document I write every sentence on a separate line. If you look straight down it'll look like a jagged edge." If every sentence ends at the same point on the page → monotonous → churn.

**When to use:** Visual review of your script document before recording. The right edge of your script should look like a stock chart, not a wall.

---

### Conversational Tone — the friend-photo tactic (from 02-master-storyteller)
**Definition:** "The most successful creators in every discipline are the ones with the most conversational tone."

**Tactic (absurdly specific, which is why it's memorable):** "Print out a picture of [a close friend] and tape it on the bottom of your camera lens... write your scripts like you're typing a text or recording an audio note directly to them."

**Examples cited:** Emma Chamberlain, Casey Neistat, Steve Jobs (first iPhone keynote 2008).

**When to use:** Before recording any talking-head. Tape the friend's photo. Speak to them, not the camera.

---

### The Last Dab (from 02-master-storyteller)
**Definition:** "The best place to start when you're writing a story is the end."

**Mechanism:** Short-form loops, so "the last line is actually a setup for the first line as it replays." Baseball metaphor: "The nine-hitter is supposed to set the table for the top of the order."

**Verbatim example:** Christopher Nolan reverse-engineers from the ending (Tenet, Inception, Interstellar).

**When to use:** When writing shorts. Write the final line first. Make it loop back to the hook.

## Discovery / research frameworks

### PBL 5x Outlier Rule (from 05-10x-faster, 07-killer-short, 08-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** The single measurable filter for "is this video worth swiping from?"

**Rule:** A "viral outlier" = a video that has **5x the views compared to that creator's follower count**, organic (not paid).

Example: a creator with 10K followers whose video did 50K views = outlier (5x). A creator with 1M followers whose video did 2M = not an outlier (2x).

**Why it works:** It controls for the creator's existing audience reach. A 50K view video from a 10K creator is signal. A 50K view video from a 1M creator is noise.

**When to use:** As the gatekeeping filter for your hook swipe file and script structure swipe file. If it doesn't pass 5x, don't add it.

---

### Monthly IG Hook Research Workflow (PBL / Ava 5-step, from 08-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** A repeatable, tap-by-tap process for finding a month's worth of proven hooks in your niche.

**Steps:**
1. **Build a keyword list** — broad niche + subtopics + occupation/title (for Gray: "video editor," "videography," "cinematography," "color grading," "wedding videographer," etc.)
2. **Reset Instagram algorithm on a dummy account** — Profile → ☰ menu → Content Preferences → Reset Suggested Content. (This matters because of step 5.)
3. **Keyword research** — search each keyword on Explore, save every video that passes the 5x rule.
4. **Account research (four sub-methods):**
   - (a) After keyword search, switch to the **Accounts** tab — screenshot every niche-fit account.
   - (b) Reels tab after keyword search — find creators that way.
   - (c) **Instagram's Suggested Accounts** — follow one good niche account, check who Instagram suggests next.
   - (d) The **Following tab** of accounts you like — peers follow peers.
   - For each saved account: Reels → drop-down → **Most Viewed** → save the outliers.
5. **Reels-tab research on the now-niche-trained dummy account** — the algorithm now serves hidden gems you'd never find via search.

**Why platform = Instagram:** "I do all my research on Instagram for hooks simply because it typically is the hardest to go viral on Instagram with short form content. So if they did go viral, you really know they're good." Hardest platform = highest signal.

**When to use:** Once a month. Block 2-3 hours. Build a Notion or Google Sheet with columns for verbal hook, written hook, visual hook, source link, structure category.

### 7-Angles × 2-Modes Matrix (from 07-killer-short and 08-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** Multiplier for turning one topic into a content batch. Already covered above under PBL 7 Hook Angles.

**Matrix:** 7 angles (Tutorial, Comparison, Mythbust, Do/Don't, Tip/Hack, Transformation, Challenge) × 2 modes (Educational, Storytelling) = **14 distinct videos per topic.**

**Applied to one Gray topic ("color grading"):**
- Tutorial: "How to color-grade in DaVinci in 5 steps"
- Comparison: "LUTs vs manual grading — which actually looks better?"
- Mythbust: "You don't need an expensive monitor"
- Do/Don't: "The 3 things every editor gets wrong in their grade"
- Tip/Hack: "This one slider changed my entire workflow"
- Transformation: "Before vs after a color grade"
- Challenge: "I color-graded 5 clips in 5 minutes"
- Then deliver each as both educational AND storytelling = 14 videos.

**When to use:** As soon as a topic enters your idea bank. Run it through the matrix before deciding which angle to film.

### 11-Format Catalog (from 07-killer-short)
**Definition:** Eleven film formats to rotate through. **"Sitting down and talking videos are boring. Okay, that worked in 2022."**

1. **Talking back-and-forth** (you in two positions/angles)
2. **Visual** — props, demos
3. **Voiceover** (talking over B-roll)
4. **Multitasking** (talking while doing something — editing, cooking, walking)
5. **Setting change** (new room/location every shot)
6. **Shot / angle change** — new shot every 2 seconds
7. **Clone** — double yourself on screen
8. **Whiteboard**
9. **Q&A**
10. **Green screen**
11. **Reaction**

**Recommendation:** Pick 2-3 you rotate. For Gray: Clone (old me vs new me editor takes), Green screen (reacting to bad edits), Multitasking (explaining while editing).

**When to use:** Pre-production. Before you script, pick the format. The format itself drives retention at least as much as the script.

### Kallaway Niche-Study Process (from 05-10x-faster)
**Definition:** The diagnostic process for picking which hook format and which story structure to use in YOUR niche.

**Verbatim:** "Just pick the hook formats that are already working for your niche and remix those. Don't over-complicate it... There is always a pattern. You will see it right away."

**Process:**
1. Pull the top 10-20 videos in your niche (use 5x rule).
2. Categorize each: which of the 9 hook formats does it use? Which of the 7 story structures?
3. The pattern will be obvious within 20 videos.
4. Pick the dominant format. Remix.

**When to use:** Once per niche, once per quarter to refresh. The pattern shifts seasonally.

### Shock Score 1-100 (from 05-10x-faster)
**Definition:** A 1-100 score for any fact: how many out of 100 people in your audience have already heard this?

**Formula:** If 4 out of 5 people haven't heard it = shock score 80.

**Rule:** Only include facts with **high shock score** in your script. Low shock = base knowledge = won't beat expectations = viewer leaves.

**The Law of Interesting:** "Interestingness comes from shock value. Shock value is the distance between what someone already knows about a topic and what you tell them. The greater this distance, the greater the shock and the more they will hold on to see why that shocking claim might be true."

**When to use:** Research stage. As you gather facts for a video, score each one. Throw out anything under 60.

## Production / scripting frameworks

### PBL 4-Step Steal-and-Templatize Workflow (from 05-10x-faster — Note: PBL 2026 video)
**Definition:** The fastest way to turn a proven viral video into a reusable script template you own.

**Steps:**
1. **Find** the outlier video (passes 5x rule) with a structure you like.
2. **Transcribe** — Three dots → Copy link → paste into **gettranscribe.ai** → get full transcript.
3. **Templatize via ChatGPT** — use this **verbatim prompt:**
   > *"This is a transcript from a viral video. Please make it into a script template that can be used for any niche. Keep the overall format/structure of the video and just make it the fill-in-the-blank version."*
   Then paste the transcript.
4. **Bank it** — save the resulting template to a Google Doc. Build a "bank" of templates for yourself and clients.

**When to use:** Whenever you spot a 5x outlier whose structure you can re-skin. Build the bank passively over months — every time you scroll, save 3 templates.

---

### Kallaway 5-Step Script Process (from 01-killer-script)
**Definition:** The order in which to build a script. Order matters — don't skip ahead.

**Steps (in order):**
1. **Packaging** — idea + title + thumbnail.
2. **Outline** — body points BEFORE intro. "Anybody can learn how to write a compelling intro — it's just a few Lego bricks. Uniqueness lives in the body."
3. **Intro** — apply the 5-Part Intro Hook framework.
4. **Body** — apply the Value Loop and "second best first" ordering.
5. **Outro** — recap, remind of pain solved, end on high note.

**Sub-framework — The Packaging Trio:**
- **Idea:** one-line pain point or rabbit hole.
- **Title:** must be locked before scripting.
- **Thumbnail:** can stay loose (contrarian to standard YT advice).

**Measure Twice, Cut Once:** Lock idea + title + outline before writing a single script line.

**When to use:** Every long-form video. This is the build order. Always.

---

### Kallaway 6-Step Script Writing System (the 32-min teardown, from 05-10x-faster)
**Definition:** The full end-to-end blueprint that supersedes the 5-step process for batched production.

**Steps:**
1. Research → write down all bullets with high shock-score facts.
2. Write desired emotion at the top of the page (filter from the 6 emotion buckets).
3. Pick hook format (study niche), tweak, screen against the 4 Hook Commandments.
4. Pick story structure (same niche-study process), write its outline, slot in facts.
5. Write the script — "layering the facts in order and adding transitions to smooth it out."
6. Read through with emotional filter — does it drive the intended emotion?

**The 4-Question Finished-Script Checklist:**
1. Is this story interesting?
2. Is this story as compressed as it can possibly be?
3. Does this hook actually hook me on its own?
4. What emotion do I feel when I finish reading?

**When to use:** This is the master process. Use the 5-step process for one-off long-forms; use this 6-step process when batching content.

### The Value Loop (per body point, from 01-killer-script)
**Definition:** A 3-part rhythm to apply to every body point in your script. Maps to how the brain processes new info.

**Formula:**
- **Context (What)** — what the point IS, simple explanation.
- **Application (How)** — how to use it, with relevant examples.
- **Framing (Why)** — why it matters, how it fits the overall story.

**When to use:** Every body point in every video. After writing each point, audit: did I include all three layers? Most beginners skip Framing — that's where retention dies.

---

### What / Why / How (outline layer, from 01-killer-script)
**Definition:** Different layering than Value Loop — applied at the outline stage to each bullet.

For each outline point:
- **What it is**
- **Why it matters**
- **How it fits the puzzle**

**When to use:** Outline review. If a bullet doesn't have all three layers, it's incomplete.

---

### Second Best First (the cheat code, from 01-killer-script)
**Definition:** Put your second-best body point first, your BEST point second, third-best third.

**Mechanism:** "Human brains work in patterns. If you show someone one thing that's amazing, they'll think it's cool. But if you show them two things that are amazing, especially if the second is better than the first, it creates a subconscious pattern where they have to stay to see the third."

**Music industry analogy:** "The best song on an album is usually track 3 or 4, never track 1."

**First-Body-Point Rule:** "The first point in the body — the first thing they hear after the intro — has to be amazing. It has to be so good that it blows their mind." If you put a weak first point, viewers skim the timestamps in the description, find nothing unique, and leave.

**When to use:** Outline finalization. After you've ranked your body points by quality, swap #1 and #2.

---

### Atomic Messaging (5 sub-tactics, from 03-cant-stop-watching)
**Definition:** "Compressing my complex ideas into the simplest form so that everyone can understand them."

**Five sub-tactics:**
1. **Simpler words** — 6th-grade vocab target. Hack: paste into Claude/ChatGPT and ask it to rewrite at 6th-grade reading level.
2. **Fewer words** — short, punchy, staccato sentences.
3. **Active voice** — *"The dog jumped"* not *"The jumping was done by the dog."*
4. **Restate twice** — define inline. *"I went to use the word rudimentary, but not everybody knows that word. So I added 'or simple' after."*
5. **Name your frameworks** — coin a term, put a stake in the ground. *"Atomic messaging," "story flow," "the dance," "the last dab," "story lenses"* — these all only work because they have names.

**Meta-tactic — Name your frameworks:** "People can relate to a single word name more than a long definition." This is why this entire document exists — every concept has been named.

**Applied to Gray:** Don't say "the way you should think about edit pacing." Coin a term — "The Pulse Edit," "The Rule of Three Cuts," "The Wedding Day Spine." Stake your claim.

**When to use:** Final script polish. Every line. Cut, simplify, rename.

---

### Staccato Sentences + Rhythm Curve (from 04-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** Force max clarity by writing short sentences early, longer ones later.

**Verbatim:** "Staccato is a type of note in music that is short. T-T. That's staccato. Shorter sentences force you to achieve max clarity. It increases the density of value per word at the beginning."

**Pattern:** Short → medium → longer as the video extends.

**When to use:** First 10 seconds of any video. 4-8 word sentences only. Open every hook with staccato.

---

### Compress Speed-to-Value (from 04-irresistible-hooks)
**Definition:** "When you make a piece of content you should feel like you have a timer counting down in your head before that content explodes and no one pays attention to it."

**Timers:**
- **Shorts:** 4 seconds to first value hit.
- **YouTube:** 1-2 minutes to first value hit.

**Verbatim principle:** "Anything you make after they click off might as well be a black screen because they're not going to see it. You should frontload the value as much as possible."

**Ideal:** Inject first hit of value *inside* the hook. Fallback: immediately after.

**Frontload, don't complete:** "You're not going for full completion — your super fans will watch the full video. You want to frontload the value as much as possible to eke out an extra 30 seconds at a time."

**When to use:** Hook writing. Always.

---

### Tell-it-to-a-Friend shortcut (from 05-10x-faster)
**Definition:** Compression test for any script.

**Verbatim:** "Imagine you were telling this story to a friend. You now know the facts you want to tell them. You now know how you would intro the story with the hook. How would you order those facts so they would understand it the most? What order do they go in?"

**When to use:** Outline review. If you'd be embarrassed to say the script out loud to a real friend, it's not ready.

---

### Native Embed CTAs (from 01-killer-script)
**Definition:** CTAs framed as solves, not as ads. Pre-planned at the outline stage to feel natural.

**Two patterns:**
- **Pattern A:** Open a loop with a pain point → offer your resource as the solve.
- **Pattern B:** Speed-run a framework too fast to absorb → offer your resource as the recap / compression tool.

**Rule:** Pre-decide in the outline which body point hosts the native embed. Don't bolt it on at the end.

**Triple-CTA close pattern (from 04-irresistible-hooks):**
1. Tied back to teaching (this was Phase 1, you still need help).
2. Soft pitch — product / waitlist with mild scarcity.
3. Free resources — subscribe / freebie grab.

**When to use:** Long-form. Place 1-2 native embeds in the body, 1 in the outro. Never start a video with a CTA.

---

### Outro / End-on-a-High-Note (from 01-killer-script)
**Definition:** "Don't treat the outro as an afterthought." Summarize points, remind viewer of pain point solved, reinforce that reality beat expectations.

**Restaurant analogy:** "The outro is the free chocolate that the restaurant gives you when they bring the check."

**5-part outro (from Kallaway's own video):**
1. Recap statement.
2. Reminder of depth covered.
3. Status elevation ("this was a full master class").
4. Engagement ask (comment / community).
5. Sign-off.

**When to use:** Every long-form. For shorts, condense to: recap line + loop-back to hook (the Last Dab).

### The 7-Factor Shorts Framework (from 07-killer-short)
**Definition:** Every short consists of these 7 components. Sanity-check before posting.

1. **Topic** — niche + sub-niche.
2. **Hooks** — verbal + written + visual (Hook Stack).
3. **Value** — measurable, not fluffy.
4. **Script angle** — one of the 7 PBL angles.
5. **CTA** — ManyChat trigger word in the comments, NOT "link in bio."
6. **Format** — one of the 11 film formats, NOT sit-and-talk.
7. **Editing style** — locked: 2-3 colors, 2-3 fonts, 2-3 visual types.

**Fluffy vs Measurable Value:**
- **Fluffy:** unclear, not specific. *"Just be authentic on camera."*
- **Measurable:** specific, actionable, clear. *"Move your camera 6 inches closer to your face — it changes everything."*

**ManyChat over link-in-bio:** Trigger word in comments (under 5-7 letters) → auto-DM the link. Boosts comments (algo signal) AND captures the lead.

**Editing style — decide once, execute forever:** 2-3 colors locked, 2-3 fonts locked (primary for hooks, secondary for captions, optional accent), 2-3 visual types locked (B-roll, screenshots, on-screen text — whatever you pick).

**When to use:** Pre-flight checklist for every short. If any of the 7 is missing, rebuild.

### Trends are just script structures (from 06-killer-script-2026)
**Definition:** Reframe of how trends actually work.

**Verbatim:** "All trends are actually just repeatable script structures. And this is exactly how they're created, whether people really understand that or not."

**Mechanism:** What looks like a "trend" is really a script structure that went viral and got remixed across niches. The audio/visual aesthetic is secondary — the underlying script template is the actual viral payload.
