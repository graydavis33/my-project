# Documentary Long-Form Template
*Week-of-footage raw documentary with talking-head A-roll inserts. Minimal edits except the first 30 seconds.*

---

## When to use this template

Use this template when you are making a **long-form, week-of-footage documentary** — not a scripted sit-down. The format looks like this:

- You filmed yourself across a real week (a client project, a build, a trip, a launch, a behind-the-scenes process).
- 90% of the cut is raw, in-the-moment footage with minimal edits — natural sound, natural pace, you walking through real situations.
- The **only heavily edited section is the first 30 seconds** — that is where you earn the right to keep the viewer watching.
- You drop in **talking-head A-roll inserts** (you sitting down, looking at the camera) at specific moments — to bridge missing footage, add context the live footage cannot, or reflect on what just happened.

**When NOT to use this template:**
- Pure sit-down talking heads (use the talking-head template instead — Sharran / Hormozi style).
- Shorts under 90 seconds (use the shorts template — there is no "week arc," just one hook + one micro-arc).
- Pure tutorial / educational content where the "story" is just a process. Documentary needs an actual narrative arc with stakes, surprises, and a payoff.

**Beginner-friendly translation:**
*A-roll* = footage of you talking directly to the camera (the "main character" footage). In a documentary, A-roll inserts are the sit-down "narrator" segments you cut to between raw clips.
*B-roll* = supporting footage — the actual week-of-footage in this case (driving to the shoot, gear on the table, the client meeting, etc.).
*Hook* = the first 5-30 seconds. The job of the hook is to make the viewer commit to watching the next minute. If the hook fails, nothing else matters.
*Rehook* = a mini-hook in the middle of the video that re-aggravates the viewer's curiosity so they don't bail. *(See 03-cant-stop-watching for the full Hook → Body → Rehook → Body → Outro architecture.)*

## The first 30 seconds (the only edited part)

This is the only section of the documentary where you go full Kallaway — punchy cuts, on-screen text, visual hooks, music. Everything from second 31 onward is raw footage with maybe a music bed and color correction. The first 30 seconds is your **one shot** to set the contract: *here is what this week was, here is the surprise, here is why you should care.*

You have two formulas to pick from. Pick **one** — do not mash them together.

### Formula A — Kallaway's 3-Step Hook (Context Lean → Scroll Stop → Contrarian Snapback)
*From 04-irresistible-hooks. Use this when your week has a clear contrarian twist — something that defies what the viewer expects.*

**Fill-in skeleton (4-7 seconds total, voiceover over edited B-roll montage):**

```
[CONTEXT LEAN — 1-2 sentences]
This past week I [WHAT YOU DID — e.g., "spent 6 days shooting a wedding for a couple who'd been told they couldn't afford a videographer"].

[SCROLL STOP INTERJECTION — 1 sentence with a contrast word: but / however / yet / although / therefore]
But here's the thing — [WHAT MOST PEOPLE WOULD EXPECT — e.g., "most editors would tell you that's a discount job you should walk away from"].

[CONTRARIAN SNAPBACK — 1 sentence that flips the expectation]
[THE TWIST — e.g., "It turned out to be the single best business decision I made all year, and I'm going to show you exactly why."]
```

**Concrete example for Gray (videographer documentary):**
> "This past week I spent 6 days inside a $40K wedding shoot with a videographer who's been doing this for 12 years. But here's the thing — most people think the secret is gear. It turns out 90% of his edge comes from one boring 90-minute habit he does every Monday morning, and by the end of this week I had stolen it."

### Formula B — Kallaway's 5-Part Intro Hook (Context → Common Belief → Contrarian Take → Proof → Plan)
*From 01-killer-script. Use this when your week is more educational/explanatory and you need to set up a teaching frame across the whole video.*

**Fill-in skeleton (15-25 seconds total):**

```
[PART 1 — IMMEDIATE CONTEXT / click confirmation — 1 sentence]
Today I'm taking you inside [WHAT YOU FILMED — e.g., "a week of working with a 7-figure videographer"].

[PART 2 — COMMON BELIEF — 1 sentence]
Most editors think [PREVAILING ASSUMPTION — e.g., "the way to charge premium prices is to buy better cameras"].

[PART 3 — CONTRARIAN TAKE — 1 sentence]
But the truth is [YOUR FLIP — e.g., "the gear has almost nothing to do with it — it's the way he talks to clients before the contract is even signed"].

[PART 4 — PROOF — 1-2 sentences, name authority + receipts]
I know this because [YOUR CREDENTIAL OR THE SUBJECT'S CREDENTIAL — e.g., "I spent the entire week inside his business and watched him close three jobs at 5x what most people in our niche charge"].

[PART 5 — PLAN — 1 sentence, name the structure of the week]
So in this video I'm going to walk you through [THE NUMBERED ARC OF THE WEEK — e.g., "the 5 days, the 3 moments that changed how I think about pricing, and the one mistake I made on day 4 that almost killed the whole shoot"].
```

### Hook hygiene — non-negotiables for the first 30 seconds

These come from 02-master-storyteller, 04-irresistible-hooks, 07-killer-short, 08-irresistible-hooks. Run every documentary intro against this checklist:

1. **Visual hook in frame 1.** Before any words land, the viewer must see something arresting. Not your face talking — an object, a result, a moment of conflict, a number on screen. *(From 02-master-storyteller: "Visual hooks are ten times more effective than audio-only hooks." Kevin from Epic Gardening's red strawberry is the canonical example.)*
2. **3-5 word text overlay on screen.** Big, bold, reinforcing the topic. *(From 04-irresistible-hooks. Kallaway's "Future of Home Design" → "Life-Size Floor Plans" 15M-view example.)*
3. **The hook stack must align.** Verbal hook + written hook + visual hook all pointing at the same idea inside 3 seconds. *(From 07-killer-short, 08-irresistible-hooks.)*
4. **No throat-clearing.** No "what's up guys," no "before we get started," no intro music longer than 1 second. Be in the topic by word 4.
5. **Click confirmation.** The first sentence should make the viewer feel "yes, I clicked the right thing." Whatever the title/thumbnail promised, the hook delivers it immediately, then beats it. *(From 01-killer-script: Mr Beast "unprecedented access" example.)*
6. **Speed to value — 4-second timer.** First hit of value lands inside 4 seconds for the in-feed scroller. *(From 04-irresistible-hooks: "Anything you make after they click off might as well be a black screen.")*
7. **Apply the Shock Score test.** Before posting, ask: "out of 100 people in my audience, how many have heard this before?" If 4 out of 5 already know it, you do not have a hook. *(From 05-10x-faster.)*

## The week arc (story structure across a week of footage)

This is where you use Kallaway's **"Dance" (Context ↔ Conflict)** framework from 02-master-storyteller, but stretched across a week instead of compressed into 30 seconds. The week is your macro arc.

> "All great stories are like a dance between context and conflict. You give a little context — the characters on a mission, they're doing something — and then, oh oh no, here comes a conflict." — Kallaway

**Beginner translation:** every day of your week should either set up stakes or break them. If two days in a row are "and then I did this, and then I did this," you have a problem. You need a "but" or a "therefore" between every day.

### The 7-day macro arc (rising stakes + payoff)

Think of your week like a 5-act story with rising tension. Even if you film 7 days, your edited cut probably has 5-6 narrative beats. Map them like this:

| Day / Beat | What it does narratively | Storytelling move |
|---|---|---|
| **Day 1 — Setup** | Establish the mission, the goal, what you're trying to figure out / build / prove. Lowest stakes. | Context. "I'm going to spend this week doing X to find out if Y." |
| **Day 2 — First conflict (small)** | Something goes wrong, harder than expected, or a surprise lands. Small stakes. | But. *(But/Therefore from 02-master-storyteller.)* |
| **Day 3 — Therefore (action)** | You respond / adjust / try a new tactic because of Day 2. | Therefore. |
| **Day 4 — Bigger conflict / the gut-punch** | The "second-best-content" slot from 01-killer-script. The day where things genuinely could go wrong. This is your **mid-doc rehook anchor**. | Big But. |
| **Day 5 — Resolution attempt / new info** | You try the fix, get new info, or hit a turning point. | Therefore. |
| **Day 6-7 — Climax + payoff (best content of the week)** | This is your BEST footage / biggest reveal. Per 01-killer-script ("Second Best First"), you put your second-best moment up front (around Day 2-3) and save your best moment for the climax. | Resolution + reveal. |
| **Closing reflection** | Talking-head A-roll insert. What you learned. The "free chocolate" outro from 01-killer-script. | Outro / payoff. |

### Three rules for sequencing your week footage

1. **Second-best first, best last.** *(From 01-killer-script.)* When you sit down to edit, find the two most shocking / emotional / surprising moments of the week. The **second-most-shocking** goes in Day 2 or Day 3 of the cut (right after the hook). The **most-shocking** goes near the end as the payoff. This creates an ascending pattern that locks the viewer in. Music industry analogy: "the best track on an album is usually #3 or #4, never #1."

2. **Run the But/Therefore audit on every cut.** *(From 02-master-storyteller.)* Watch your assembly and ask between every clip: does this connect with "but" or "therefore"? If the only word that fits is "and then," that clip is disposable B-roll, not story. Cut it. *(02-master-storyteller's South Park rule.)*

3. **Pre-decide the story lens BEFORE you shoot.** *(From 02-master-storyteller.)* Most documentaries fail because the creator filmed everything and tried to find the story later. Decide your "prism" before Day 1 — what angle no one else would take on this week? Kallaway covered Taylor Swift at the Super Bowl by looking at her *business impact on the NFL* — got 1M views. For Gray: instead of "a week with a wedding videographer," try "a week proving wedding videography is actually a sales business with a camera on the side." That's a lens. Now your footage has a thesis to point at.

### The Story Flow Audit (zero "stable upbringing" tangents)

*From 03-cant-stop-watching:* the single biggest mistake in raw documentary editing is including every cool moment that happened, even if it doesn't serve the through line. Kallaway's prince-in-the-forest example — the prince gets distracted by a fox, then a goat, then his stable upbringing, and you've lost the audience.

**Clip-by-clip audit question (steal verbatim):**
> "Is this clip a necessary piece of context, or an intentional step in the logic flow that gets the viewer's understanding to go up? Or is it a distraction and an unnecessary detail?"

If the honest answer is "distraction," cut it — even if you love the shot. *(From 03-cant-stop-watching: "Great storytelling is like hypnosis. The more red herrings or unnecessary details you throw in, the more fragile and surface level that hypnosis becomes.")*

## Talking-head A-roll insert template

This is the heart of the documentary format. The week of raw footage is the body of the story; the A-roll inserts are the **narration spine** that holds it together. Use them deliberately — not as filler.

### When to use a talking-head insert (4 valid reasons)

1. **Bridge missing footage.** You didn't have the camera rolling when the important thing happened, or the footage is unusable (audio, lighting, you weren't there). Use an A-roll insert to narrate what happened.
2. **Add context the live footage cannot show.** Background info, a stat, a number, a credential, a quote from someone not on camera, a comparison to the broader industry.
3. **Reflect / process / set up the next beat.** A reflective A-roll insert lets you slow down, give the viewer space to absorb what just happened, and tee up what's coming.
4. **Rehook the viewer.** Insert at the moment you sense viewers will start to drift (around the midpoint, especially after a slower section). See the next section for rehook beats.

**When NOT to use an A-roll insert:**
- Just because the raw footage feels slow. (Better fix: cut the raw footage tighter.)
- To explain a joke or a moment that should speak for itself.
- More than once every 3-5 minutes in a long documentary. They should feel like rare, important anchors — not constant interruptions.

### How to film A-roll inserts (production discipline)

- **Film them all on the same day, in the same outfit, in the same setup.** This is the biggest beginner mistake — filming inserts day-by-day during the week means lighting and wardrobe will be inconsistent, which kills the documentary feel. Film inserts AFTER the week is done, when you know what story you're telling.
- **Different background from your normal sit-down talking head.** Documentary inserts should feel like "behind the scenes" — desk, edit suite, kitchen counter, B-roll location — not the polished studio setup.
- **Looser, more conversational delivery.** Documentary inserts are not lectures. Talk like you're explaining the week to a friend at a bar.
- **Script every insert but read it aloud first.** Use Kallaway's **jagged-edge rhythm test** from 02-master-storyteller: write each sentence on its own line. If every line ends at the same point on the page, it'll sound monotonous on camera. Vary length: short. Short. Medium length sentence. Then a longer sentence that breathes a little and lets the idea land. Short again.

### Specific opener phrasings for A-roll inserts (steal these)

These are the "transition words" that tell the viewer "we're cutting away from the live footage so I can explain something." Pick the one that matches the insert's job.

**For BRIDGING missing footage:**
- "Okay, so what you didn't see was..."
- "I couldn't film this part, but here's what actually happened..."
- "Off camera, [X] happened — and that's the reason [next beat]."
- "Quick context before this next part lands..."

**For ADDING CONTEXT:**
- "Before we get to [next moment], I have to tell you something about [topic]."
- "I should explain why this matters."
- "If you don't know [X], this next part isn't going to hit. So real quick..."
- "Here's what I learned later that changes how you watch this..."

**For REFLECTING after a beat:**
- "Okay, let me step back for a second."
- "I didn't realize it at the time, but..."
- "Looking back at the footage now, [insight]."
- "If I could go back and redo this day, I'd [X]." *(This one is especially good because it teaches by counterfactual.)*

**For SETTING UP THE NEXT BEAT (rehook flavor):**
- "What I'm about to show you almost didn't make the final cut..."
- "This next part is the one I want you to pay attention to."
- "Here's where the week turned."
- "Stay with me on this — what happens in the next two days is the whole reason I made this video."

### Transition phrases BACK TO live footage (don't just cut blind)

After the A-roll insert ends, transition back to the week footage with a phrase that re-engages the viewer:

- "Okay, back to [day / moment]."
- "So with all that in mind, here's what happened next..."
- "Watch this." *(Then cut to the live footage.)*
- "Roll the footage." *(Casual, signals "I'm done narrating, here's the real moment.")*

## Mid-doc rehook beats

A **rehook** is a mini-hook in the middle of the video that re-aggravates curiosity so the viewer doesn't bail. *(Defined in 03-cant-stop-watching: "the reaggravation of the painpoint originally triggered in the hook by showing that there's a better solution still out there. Hey, what I said is good, but what's coming next is great.")*

In a scripted talking head, rehooks are explicit verbal cues ("now this next point is the most important one"). **In a documentary, rehooks look different.** You have three rehook surfaces to work with.

### Rehook surface 1: A-roll insert as rehook

The most powerful rehook in a doc is a 5-10 second talking-head insert dropped right before your best footage. The job: re-aggravate the original promise and set up the climax.

**Verbal cue templates (steal verbatim):**
- "Okay, everything you've watched so far was setup. **This next part is the whole reason I made this video.**"
- "What I'm about to show you is the moment that changed the entire week."
- "If you only watch one part of this, watch this next 90 seconds."
- "I almost didn't include this on camera. Now I'm glad I did."
- "Remember at the start I told you [hook promise]? Here's where that pays off."
- "That last day was important. **But if you don't pair it with what happened next, the whole story falls apart.**" *(Direct adaptation of Kallaway's classic rehook line from 01-killer-script: "That point was super important, but if you don't also couple it with this one, then the magic is lost.")*

### Rehook surface 2: On-screen text rehook (no voiceover)

Hold one frame of the raw footage. Drop big bold text on screen for 2-3 seconds. Then cut back to the footage. Use this when you don't want to break the documentary feel with a sit-down insert.

Examples:
- **"This is where it all changed."**
- **"Watch what happens next."**
- **"The part nobody believes."**
- **"Day 4 — everything almost fell apart."**

### Rehook surface 3: A "callback" to the hook

Halfway through the doc, cut briefly back to the same B-roll shot you used in the opening hook, with new context. This works as a curiosity loop — viewer remembers "oh right, the opening teased this." *(See 04-irresistible-hooks: Kallaway opens with "the screen is the least impressive part of the sphere" at 3:30, closes the loop at 6:15.)*

### When to drop a rehook (timing rules)

- **In a 10-20 minute doc:** rehook every 3-5 minutes. *(Per 03-cant-stop-watching's "2-3 minutes for YouTube" rule, slightly longer for documentary pacing because raw footage breathes more than scripted.)*
- **The single most important rehook is at the 40-50% mark** — this is the bail point where viewers who weren't fully hooked will leave.
- **Always rehook right before your best footage (the climax).** That's the moment you can least afford a churn.

## Closing the story (CTA + cliffhanger to next week)

The outro is where most creators undo all their hard work. Don't.

### The 4-part documentary outro (adapted from 01-killer-script + 02-master-storyteller)

1. **Recap statement (10-15 seconds).** Talking-head A-roll insert. Restate what the week was, what you learned, what changed. *(From 01-killer-script: "summarize, remind of pain point solved, reinforce that reality beat expectations.")*

   Skeleton:
   > "So that was [week summary]. The biggest thing I took away was [main insight]. Going in I thought [original belief]. Coming out, [new belief]."

2. **The Last Dab (the loop-back line).** *(From 02-master-storyteller: "The best place to start when you're writing a story is the end. The last line is actually a setup for the first line as it replays.")* Even though documentaries don't loop on auto-play the way shorts do, a great last line gets viewers to feel the satisfaction of closure AND quote the line in comments / share. Write this line FIRST when you start the doc.

   Skeleton:
   > "[One sentence that lands the lesson and could be screenshot-quotable.]"
   > Example: *"Turns out the cheapest gear in his studio was the most important one — his calendar."*

3. **Cliffhanger to next week (the documentary CTA superpower).** This is what makes the documentary format generate channel watch-time. Tease next week's documentary at the end of this one.

   Skeleton:
   > "Next week I'm [WHAT YOU'RE DOING — e.g., 'spending 7 days with a creator who built a 6-figure business in 11 months using just an iPhone']. **You're going to want to see this one.** Make sure you're subscribed."

   This is your strongest CTA — better than "smash like" because it gives the viewer a concrete, dated reason to come back.

4. **Soft community / resource CTA (optional).** *(From 05-10x-faster and 02-master-storyteller — both end on community plugs, not subscribe-spam.)*

   Skeleton:
   > "If you want the [specific resource — e.g., 'cold-email template he showed me on Day 3'], comment '[trigger word]' below and I'll send it. Otherwise, see you next week."

   *(For shorts, swap this for a ManyChat trigger word per 07-killer-short. For long-form docs, a free resource + soft community plug works better than a product pitch.)*

### What NOT to do in the outro

- Don't pitch a product hard. Save product pitches for native embeds mid-doc, not the outro. *(From 01-killer-script: outro is "the free chocolate the restaurant gives you when they bring the check" — leave the viewer feeling like they got something free, not pitched at.)*
- Don't dump 4 CTAs. One cliffhanger + one soft resource CTA. Maximum.
- Don't end on subscribe-bait. End on a quotable line. Subscribe-bait is the audio equivalent of clickbait.
