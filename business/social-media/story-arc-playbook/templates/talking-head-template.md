# Sit-Down Talking-Head Long-Form Template

This is the playbook for a Sharran Srivatsaa / Alex Hormozi / Kallaway style sit-down — you on camera, talking to a lens, with heavy on-screen text, B-roll, embedded proof clips, and named frameworks doing the visual heavy lifting. The script is the spine; the editing turns it into a "masterclass."

---

## When to use this template

Use this template when:

- You are teaching one big idea or a numbered system (4 mistakes, 6 tactics, 5 steps).
- You want a video that lives on YouTube long-form (8–25 minutes) and can be sliced into shorts later.
- You have receipts (case studies, client wins, your own numbers, screenshots) you can show on screen.
- You want to *coin* a framework — name the thing so people remember it and credit you.
- You are positioning yourself as an authority in the videographer/editor space.

Do **NOT** use this template when:

- You want pure storytelling/documentary energy — use the raw-doc template.
- The idea fits in under 90 seconds — make a Short instead.
- You have no proof (no clips, no examples, no receipts) — go gather proof first. From `06-killer-script-2026`: "If you're just repeating existing base knowledge, you will not be able to beat expectations with your reality."

## The 5-part intro hook (Kallaway formula, lift verbatim)

Every sit-down opens with the same 5-part intro hook. From `01-killer-script` and `06-killer-script-2026` — this is the formula Kallaway uses on every video. Beats 4 and 5 (proof + plan) can swap order.

| # | Part | What it does | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Immediate context** | First sentence names the topic. Confirms the click. The right viewer leans in, the wrong viewer leaves. | 0–4s |
| 2 | **Common belief** | State what most people think about this topic. Builds shared ground. | 4–10s |
| 3 | **Contrarian take** | Flip it. State your opposing view. This is the "stun gun" — from `04-irresistible-hooks` use words like *but, however, yet, although, therefore*. | 10–18s |
| 4 | **Proof / credibility** | Drop your numbers. Followers, views, years, clients, results. No humble-bragging detour — just facts. | 18–28s |
| 5 | **Plan** | Tell them exactly what's coming. Use a number ("6 tips," "4 mistakes," "3 steps"). Finite = followable. | 28–40s |

**Beginner note on "contrarian take":** Contrarian doesn't mean inflammatory. It just means *not the conventional answer.* If everyone says "post 3x a day," your contrarian take could be "post once a week if every post is a banger." It's the angle most creators in your niche aren't taking.

### Fill-in template (use this verbatim, then customize)

```
Today we're talking about [TOPIC].

If you want [DESIRED OUTCOME], you need to [WHAT THEY NEED].

Now most people think [COMMON BELIEF].

But here's the thing — [CONTRARIAN TAKE].

I know this works because I've [PROOF / RECEIPTS — numbers, clients, results].

So in this video I'm going to break down the [NUMBER]-[step/tip/mistake]
framework for [PROMISED OUTCOME]. Let's dive in.
```

### One full example (videographer audience)

> Today we're talking about getting clients as a wedding videographer. If you want couples to actually book you instead of ghosting after the discovery call, you need to fix one specific thing on your website. Now most people think the fix is better testimonials or a fancier reel. But here's the thing — your reel is probably costing you bookings, not winning them. I've shot 80 weddings, rebuilt my site three times, and the version that 4x'd my booking rate had no reel on the homepage at all. So in this video I'm going to break down the 4 mistakes 95% of wedding videographers are making on their site, and the exact 4 fixes that turned my discovery calls into signed contracts. Let's dive in.

That hits all 5 parts in roughly 35 seconds. Notice: contrarian take comes BEFORE proof. The "but here's the thing" is the stun-gun trigger from `04-irresistible-hooks`.

## Body structure: Hook → Body → Rehook → Body → Outro

From `03-cant-stop-watching`, this is the architecture for every long-form sit-down:

```
HOOK (40s)
  ↓
BODY POINT (2nd best — see next section)
  ↓
REHOOK (10–15s) — every 2–3 minutes for YouTube long-form
  ↓
BODY POINT (3rd / supporting)
  ↓
REHOOK
  ↓
BODY POINT (BEST — saved for the end)
  ↓
OUTRO + last-dab + CTA
```

### Why each beat exists

- **Hook (0–40s):** From `03-cant-stop-watching`: social media is "a lucky slot machine pool for your video to show up in front of someone for free, and you have to earn the right for them to keep watching." The hook earns the next 2 minutes.
- **Body point:** Where the actual teaching happens. Each body point is its own mini-arc: claim → proof → application. From `01-killer-script`, every body point runs the **Value Loop**: Context (what it is) → Application (how to do it) → Framing (why it matters).
- **Rehook (every 2–3 min on YouTube, every 20–25s on Shorts):** Re-aggravates the pain you opened with and teases that something better is still coming. From `03-cant-stop-watching` verbatim: *"Hey, what I said is good, but what's coming next is great."* Without rehooks, attention decays and viewers bail at the 2-minute mark.
- **Outro:** Recap + last-dab + CTA. Don't treat as afterthought. From `01-killer-script`: "The outro is the free chocolate that the restaurant gives you when they bring the check." End on a high.

**Timing rule of thumb for YouTube long-form:** rehook every 2–3 minutes. For a 12-minute video that's 3–4 rehooks. For Shorts (covered in shorts template) rehook lives at the 20–25 second mark.

## Body section ordering: second-best point first

Kallaway calls this "the cheat code" in `01-killer-script` — and it is the single most counterintuitive move in the whole playbook. The order is:

1. **Second-best point first** (right after the hook)
2. Third-best (or supporting) in the middle
3. **Best point last** (just before the outro)

### Why this beats the alternatives

**The Album Analogy (Kallaway, verbatim from `01-killer-script`):**

> "Human brains work in patterns. If you show someone one thing that's amazing, they'll think it's cool. But if you show them two things that are amazing, especially if the second is better than the first, it creates a subconscious pattern where they have to stay to see the third."

In music: the best track on an album is almost never track 1. It's track 3 or 4. Track 1 is "really good" — good enough to keep you listening. The single they push is buried deeper because the listener is already committed by then.

**Why "best first" fails:** If your first point is your best, every following point feels like a downgrade. Viewers feel the descent and leave.

**Why "chronological" fails:** Chronological is for stories, not teaching. It buries your strongest material under setup.

**Why "second best first" wins:** Second-best is still strong enough to satisfy click-confirmation. The viewer thinks "if THIS is point 1, point 3 must be insane." That's the ascending pattern that locks them in.

**One rule from `01-killer-script` that backs this up:** "The first point in the body — the first thing they hear after the intro — has to be amazing. It has to be so good that it blows their mind." Your second-best point is "amazing." Save your "holy shit" point for the end.

## On-screen text emphasis pattern

This is the visual layer that turns a regular talking-head into a Kallaway/Sharran/Hormozi-style sit-down. Every reference video in our batch uses on-screen text emphasis heavily.

### What to emphasize on screen

| Element | When it appears | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **The framework name** | The moment you name it ("The 5-Part Hook," "The Value Loop") | Names stick. From `03-cant-stop-watching`: "People can relate to a single word name more than a long definition." Put a stake in the ground. |
| **Numbered list items** | As you say "Tip 1," "Tip 2," etc. | Shows progress. Lowers bail risk. |
| **Key stats / numbers** | Anytime you say a number ("$122,000/yr," "4 mistakes," "40M views") | Numbers feel like proof. |
| **One-liner punchlines** | The exact moment you say a steal-worthy line | Burns the line into memory. From `02-master-storyteller`: "Visual hooks are 10 times more effective than audio-only hooks." |
| **Definitions inline** | When you say "or simple" — show both the big word and the simple version | From `03-cant-stop-watching`: restate twice tactic. Catches everyone in the audience. |
| **The "but" / "however" beat** | Right at the contrast moment | Visualizes the pivot. |

### When to NOT use on-screen text

- During pure storytelling beats (it competes with the story).
- During B-roll where the B-roll IS the proof — don't double up.
- When you want emphasis through silence/pause instead.

### Style rule (from Personal Brand Launch via `07-killer-short`)

Lock the style once and reuse forever:
- **2–3 colors** (your brand colors + one accent)
- **2–3 fonts** (primary for hooks, secondary for captions, optional accent)
- **One position rule** — most creators do upper-third or center. Pick one and stay there.

For Gray specifically: you already use Montserrat SemiBold in the Sai captions tool — keep that as primary for on-screen text in talking-heads too. Consistent fonts build brand recognition.

## B-roll and embedded clip placement (claim → proof clip pattern)

From `02-master-storyteller`, this is the most powerful retention mechanic in the entire batch: **every major claim is immediately followed by an embedded proof clip.**

### The pattern

```
You make a claim     →     You cut to a clip that proves it     →     You return and explain
```

### Cited examples from references

**The Kallaway South Park / NYU example (`02-master-storyteller`, Tip 1):**
Kallaway claims great stories use "but/therefore" not "and then." Then he cuts to the famous Matt Stone & Trey Parker NYU clip explaining the same rule. He's not just citing Stone & Parker — he's *outsourcing his credibility* to them for 60 seconds. When the clip ends, the viewer accepts the rule as canon.

**The Stanley Cup example (`02-master-storyteller`, Tip 1):**
Kallaway then plays his OWN viral Stanley Cup video and counts "four but-then conflict loops in the first 30 seconds." He demonstrates the technique working in a video the viewer already saw or has heard of. Self-referential proof.

**The Iron Man / Vegas Sphere examples (`04-irresistible-hooks`, `05-10x-faster`):**
Claim: "Shock value = distance between what they know and what you tell them." Proof: cuts to the Vegas Sphere reel that did 8M views, or the Russian-engineer Iron Man reel that did 40M views in 5 days. Numbers + clip = undeniable.

### When to embed which type of clip

| Claim type | What to cut to |
|---|---|
| "Top creator X does this" | A clip of that creator doing it |
| "This worked for me" | Your own viral / receipt clip with view count on screen |
| "Top experts agree" | An interview clip from a recognized authority (Stone/Parker, Nolan, Jobs) |
| "Most people get this wrong" | Stack 3–5 short clips showing the wrong version |
| "Here's a number" | Screen-recorded analytics or a screenshot with the stat highlighted |

### Gray-specific application

You shoot for videographers and editors. Your B-roll library should include:
- Screen recordings of Premiere/DaVinci with the effect you're teaching.
- Outlier reels from your niche (videographer/editor space) — pre-saved per the 5x rule from `07-killer-short` and `08-irresistible-hooks`.
- Your own client wins / before-and-afters.
- Quote cards from recognized authorities (cinematographers, MrBeast's editors, A24, Casey Neistat).

**Rule of thumb:** Don't make a claim you can't show. If you can't B-roll it, the claim is probably too vague — rewrite it.

## Mid-video rehook formulas

A rehook is a 5–15 second beat that reopens the curiosity loop right when attention is decaying. It is NOT a recap. A recap looks back; a rehook teases forward.

### The verbatim rehook templates worth stealing

**From `03-cant-stop-watching` (the master rehook template):**
> "Hey, what I said is good, but what's coming next is great."

**From `01-killer-script`:**
> "That point was super important, but if you don't also couple it with this one, then the magic is lost."

**From `05-10x-faster` (zoom-out rehook):**
> "Now let's zoom back out. At this point in the process, we've covered [X] and [Y]. The next most important piece to focus on is [Z]."

**From `01-killer-script` (curiosity-gap rehook):**
> "This next one is deep deep deep psychology that you will not hear anywhere else on YouTube."

**From `04-irresistible-hooks` (the "haymaker" rehook):**
> "All right, time for step three — this is the haymaker."

**From `02-master-storyteller` (numbered transition rehook):**
> "All right, tip number [X] is [name of next tip]."

### Rehook fill-in templates (for Gray's videographer/editor content)

Pick one of these every 2–3 minutes:

- *"That was [point you just covered]. But [point you just covered] only works if you also do this next thing — and most editors get it completely wrong."*
- *"Now if you only take one thing from this video, take this next one."*
- *"All right, this next mistake is the one that single-handedly killed my view count for 8 months."*
- *"Quick zoom out. We've covered [X] and [Y]. The next one is the one that changes everything."*
- *"Pay attention to this next part. This is where 90% of creators churn — and the ones who get it become unbeatable."*

### Why rehooks work (the mechanic, not just the line)

From `03-cant-stop-watching` verbatim:
> "Rehook = the re-aggravation of the painpoint originally triggered in the hook by showing that there's a better solution still out there."

So every rehook has two jobs:
1. **Re-aggravate the original pain** (the one from your intro hook).
2. **Tease that the next point is the bigger solution.**

If a rehook only does job 1, it feels like complaining. If it only does job 2, it feels like hype. You need both.

## The last-dab / dopamine ending

From `02-master-storyteller`, Kallaway calls this "the last dab" — the final line of the video, written BEFORE you write the rest. It is the dopamine hit that sends viewers off either rewatching, sharing, or commenting.

### Kallaway's last-dab principle (verbatim from `02-master-storyteller`)

> "The best place to start when you're writing a story is the end."
> "I want it to be so memorable that if that's all someone heard, they'd be willing to share it with a friend."

For long-form, the last-dab isn't about looping back to the hook (that's the shorts trick). It's about leaving the viewer with one final, emotionally loaded line that summarizes the transformation.

### The full outro structure (from `01-killer-script` + `05-10x-faster`)

Order matters. Every long-form outro runs in this order:

| # | Beat | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Recap statement** | "This was the [number]-step formula for [outcome]." |
| 2 | **Reminder of pain solved** | "We covered [X], [Y], and [Z] — and we even broke down [the deepest one]." |
| 3 | **Status elevation** | "This was a full master class." |
| 4 | **Mission line** (optional but powerful) | "My mission with this channel is to make it easier for [audience] to [outcome]." |
| 5 | **Last-dab line** | One memorable, shareable sentence. The "free chocolate." |
| 6 | **Soft CTA** | Comment, subscribe, join the community, grab the freebie. |
| 7 | **Sign-off** | Casual. "Peace." / "See you on the next one." / "Happy [verb]ing." |

### CTA placement rule

From `01-killer-script` — don't save your CTA for the outro. Use the **native embed** pattern:
- Pre-plan in the outline which body point naturally hosts a CTA.
- Two CTA patterns:
  - **Pattern A:** Open a loop with a pain point → offer your resource as the solve.
  - **Pattern B:** Speedrun a framework too fast to absorb → offer your resource as the recap/compression tool.
- Place the FIRST CTA around the midpoint (after Body Point 2, before Body Point 3). The outro CTA is the second/third.

The outro itself should not be the hard pitch. The outro is the celebration of value delivered. The CTA is woven in earlier.

### Last-dab examples (for Gray's audience)

- *"Most videographers spend years trying to perfect their craft. The ones who actually build a business spend that same time perfecting one of these four things. Pick yours."*
- *"You don't need better gear. You need better edits. And now you know exactly what those look like."*
- *"That's the game. Go run it."*

## Fill-in-the-blank skeleton for a 10–15 minute sit-down

Below is the full skeleton, beat-by-beat, with sample fills in italics for a hypothetical video: *"The 3 Edits That 4x'd My Client's Wedding Bookings."*

### [0:00 – 0:40] HOOK (40 seconds)

**Beat 1 — Context (4s):** *"Today we're talking about the 3 small edits that 4x'd my client's wedding bookings."*

**Beat 2 — Common belief (6s):** *"Most wedding videographers think bookings come from a better reel or a better camera."*

**Beat 3 — Contrarian (8s):** *"But here's the thing — I've now worked with 12 wedding videographers, and the ones who 4x'd their bookings didn't change their reel at all. They changed three boring things most editors completely ignore."*

**Beat 4 — Proof (10s):** *"I've edited over 200 wedding films, I've grown my own brand to [X] followers, and the system I'm about to show you took one of my clients from 6 bookings a year to 24."*

**Beat 5 — Plan (12s):** *"In this video I'm going to break down the 3 edits that did it, plus a bonus 4th one at the end that almost no one is using. Let's dive in."*

**Visual layer:** Bold on-screen text "3 EDITS = 4X BOOKINGS" in upper third. Cut to a 2-second client receipt screenshot at the proof beat. Cut to a quick montage of wedding B-roll across the whole hook.

---

### [0:40 – 3:30] BODY POINT 1 — Your SECOND-BEST point (~2.5 min)

This is the point that pays off click-confirmation. Strong, but not your strongest.

**Sample fill:** *"Edit #1: Drop the slow-motion intro from your highlight films."*

**Mini-arc inside this body point (the Value Loop from `01-killer-script`):**

1. **Context — what it is** (~30s): Define the slow-mo intro problem.
2. **Application — how to fix it** (~60s): Walk through the alternative opening, screen-record the actual edit in Premiere/DaVinci. Show the before/after.
3. **Framing — why it matters** (~30s): Tie it back to the booking pain. "When couples scroll your portfolio they're not looking for art — they're looking for a feeling they can imagine themselves inside."
4. **Embedded proof clip** (~20s): Cut to a 5x-outlier wedding reel that does it the right way. Or your own client's before/after.
5. **Mini-recap** (~10s): "So Edit 1 — kill the slow-mo intro. Easy fix, massive change."

---

### [3:30 – 3:45] REHOOK #1

*"That fix alone got my client 4 new inquiries the week we re-edited her site. But Edit 1 only works if you also do Edit 2 — and Edit 2 is the one nobody is doing."*

**Visual:** Bold text "EDIT 2 — THE ONE NOBODY IS DOING."

---

### [3:45 – 6:30] BODY POINT 2 — Your supporting / third-best point (~2.75 min)

**Sample fill:** *"Edit #2: Cut the bride-prep montage in half."*

Run the same Value Loop: context → application → framing → proof clip → mini-recap. Embed an outlier wedding video that does it well. Mid-way through this point, drop your **first native CTA** (Pattern A — open a pain loop, offer a freebie):

*"By the way, if you want my exact bride-prep timing template — the one I use for every wedding I edit — I'm giving it away free in the description. Just for watching this far. All right, back to it."*

---

### [6:30 – 6:45] REHOOK #2

*"Now if you only take one thing from this entire video, take this next one. This is the edit that single-handedly turned my client from 'booking the occasional wedding' to 'fully booked 9 months out.'"*

**Visual:** Bold text "EDIT 3 — THE GAME-CHANGER."

---

### [6:45 – 10:30] BODY POINT 3 — Your BEST point (~3.75 min)

This is your "holy shit" moment. Spend the most time here. This is what they came for.

**Sample fill:** *"Edit #3: Add a 12-second 'next-day reaction' opener to every highlight film."*

Run the Value Loop here too, but stretch it. This is where you can:
- Show 2–3 stacked proof clips (creators doing it well).
- Show your full before/after with view-count or booking-count receipts.
- Add a personal story about how you discovered this.
- Name the technique (e.g., "the Reaction Cold Open") — put a stake in the ground per `03-cant-stop-watching`.

End the section with a mini-recap that reinforces the framework name.

---

### [10:30 – 10:45] REHOOK #3 (optional — bonus tease)

*"Before I wrap up, there's a bonus 4th edit I promised you at the start of the video — and this one is so under-used I almost didn't share it. But here we go."*

---

### [10:45 – 12:00] BONUS BODY POINT (optional, ~1.25 min)

A short, dense bonus point. Doesn't have to be the strongest — its job is to pay off the "bonus at the end" promise from the hook. Keeps watch time pinned to the very end.

**Sample fill:** *"Bonus Edit #4: Add a 3-second 'price reveal' card at the very end of the film. Most editors hide pricing — but adding it eliminates the awkward email back-and-forth that loses you 30% of leads."*

---

### [12:00 – 13:00] OUTRO + last-dab + final CTA (~1 min)

**Recap:** *"All right, that was the 4 edits — kill the slow-mo intro, cut the bride-prep in half, add the reaction cold open, and end with a price card."*

**Pain solved:** *"If you do these four things, your highlight reels stop being 'pretty videos' and start being booking machines."*

**Status elevation:** *"This is essentially a free masterclass in wedding video editing for business."*

**Mission line:** *"My whole goal with this channel is to help videographers actually make a living from this craft — not just be artists waiting to be discovered."*

**Last-dab:** *"You don't need better gear. You need better edits. And now you know exactly what those look like."*

**Final soft CTA:** *"If this helped, leave a comment with which edit you're trying first — I read every one. And if you want the bride-prep template I mentioned, link's in the description. See you in the next one."*

**Sign-off:** *"Peace."* (or whatever your signature is — pick one and use it on every video for brand consistency).

### Total runtime target

| Section | Time |
|---|---|
| Hook | 0:40 |
| Body 1 (2nd best) | 2:50 |
| Rehook 1 | 0:15 |
| Body 2 (supporting) + native CTA | 2:45 |
| Rehook 2 | 0:15 |
| Body 3 (BEST) | 3:45 |
| Rehook 3 (optional) | 0:15 |
| Bonus body | 1:15 |
| Outro | 1:00 |
| **Total** | **~13:00** |

For a tighter 10-min cut, drop the bonus body point. For a 15-min cut, expand Body 3 with a second proof clip and a deeper personal story.
