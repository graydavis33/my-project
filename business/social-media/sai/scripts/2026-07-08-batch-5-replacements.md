## Video 3 (replacement) — The AI Workflow We Actually Use

Replaces: marziaprince wrong-vs-right acting demo. That one made Sai act out a cringe "wrong" version on camera. This shows instead of performs.

Status: Sai Review
Format: screen-record demo + voiceover. Sai narrates over his actual screen while an AI workflow runs. Fast, saveable, on-brand (AI-first agency is his real authority lane).
Orientation: Horizontal.

Why it fits: no acting; "show don't tell"; genuinely useful = saveable/shareable (Sai's saveable-content directive); a completely different format from anything else in the batch. Voice stays experiential — "here's the exact thing we do," not "here's how you should work."

Topics (Sai fills, one pick): the single AI workflow to demo — e.g. turning one long video into a week of posts, drafting a client report, researching a hook, editing a rough cut. Pick the one that looks most impressive on screen in under a minute.

Verbal hook:
- A: This used to take my team three hours. Now it takes about six minutes — let me show you.
- B: Everyone talks about using AI in a business. Nobody shows the actual screen. Here's ours.
- C: I'm going to do a full [task] in the time it takes to watch this.

Caption hook:
- A: WATCH ME DO [X] IN 60 SECONDS
- B: THE AI WORKFLOW WE ACTUALLY USE
- C: 3 HOURS OF WORK → 6 MINUTES

Visual hook:
- A: Cold open straight on the screen recording — cursor already moving, no face first.
- B: Split second of Sai saying the promise, then hard-cut into the screen.
- C: A timer starts in the corner as the workflow begins (beat-the-clock).

Editor brief: promise (verbal) → screen recording of the real workflow, sped up with VO callouts of each step → the finished output revealed at the end → one line on the time/effort saved. Captions house style. 30–60s. 16:9.

## Video 5 (replacement) — Where Every Dollar You Pay Us Goes

Replaces: sharransrivatsaa famous-mentor access story. That one needed Sai to name-drop a mentor and claim access as authority. This trades the flex for radical transparency — more relatable, more on-brand ("nothing to sell you").

Status: Sai Review
Format: money-flow data graphic + Sai voiceover. A stack of cash splits into labeled buckets as he talks. Build-in-public transparency.
Orientation: Horizontal.

Why it fits: no authority flex — the opposite, it's Sai being open about his own business; builds trust; a data/finance visual the batch doesn't have. Humble voice by default.

Topics (Sai fills): the rough real split of where a client's payment actually goes (team pay, tools/software, ad spend, taxes, what's left). Numbers can be ballpark/percentages — must be real-ish, not invented precision.

Verbal hook:
- A: People assume I pocket most of what a client pays me. Here's where it actually goes.
- B: A client pays us [X] a month. Let me show you what's left by the end of it.
- C: Nobody in this industry shows you this, so I will.

Caption hook:
- A: WHERE YOUR MONEY ACTUALLY GOES
- B: WHAT A RETAINER REALLY PAYS FOR
- C: WHAT'S LEFT AFTER A CLIENT PAYS US

Visual hook:
- A: A stack of cash / a full bar on screen that immediately starts splitting into labeled buckets.
- B: Sai holds up a bill, then the graphic takes over and divides it.
- C: A pie or flow graphic building live as he names each slice.

Editor brief: hook → the total → each bucket splits off with its number/percent and a one-line why → land on what's actually left (usually surprisingly little = the payoff). Captions house style. 30–45s. 16:9. Graphic built in HyperFrames, brand orange.

## Video 6 (replacement) — I Was Wrong About This

Replaces: neilpatel contrarian "X is already dead" hot take. That one breaks two rules — not-the-expert and no-disparaging (it declares everyone else obsolete). This keeps the engaging, opinionated hook but flips it to humble self-revision.

Status: Sai Review
Format: talking head + b-roll punch-ins. A belief-shift confession.
Orientation: Horizontal.

Why it fits: admitting he was wrong reads as earned authority and starts comment debates (the doc's own note on Video 4), but with zero teardown of anyone else; textbook not-the-expert voice. Still delivers a strong opinion — just aimed at his past self.

Topics (Sai fills): a real belief he held and changed — e.g. about hiring, ad spend, followers vs revenue, working more hours, a channel/platform, "more content = more growth." The bigger the reversal, the better.

Verbal hook:
- A: For two years I believed [X]. I was completely wrong, and it cost me.
- B: I used to argue this with everyone. Now I think I had it backwards.
- C: This is the belief that held my business back the longest.

Caption hook:
- A: I WAS WRONG ABOUT THIS
- B: THE BELIEF THAT COST ME THE MOST
- C: I HAD THIS BACKWARDS FOR 2 YEARS

Visual hook:
- A: Dead-eye to camera, no smile, before the first word.
- B: Text on screen states the old belief, then a hard cut / strike-through as he corrects it.
- C: B-roll of the mistake era, then cut to present-day Sai for the correction.

Editor brief: state the old belief (own it fully) → what happened / what changed his mind → what he believes now → invite disagreement ("tell me if you still think I'm wrong"). Kill music under the admission. Captions house style. 30–45s. 16:9.

## Video 10 (replacement) — The Leaky Bucket

Replaces: personalbrandlaunch business-terms listicle. That was redundant with Video 11 (Sai kept the buzzwords twin). This swaps in a completely different format: a physical-metaphor explainer, like the tier-list bit but with a prop.

Status: Sai Review
Format: physical prop / analogy demo. Sai explains one concept using a real object. Scripted around the metaphor.
Orientation: Horizontal.

Why it fits: removes the redundancy with 11; adds a memorable, visual, physical format; fully scriptable (the analogy is designed, not improvised); teaches through a concrete image, humble voice.

The pitch (locked analogy): why content / a business doesn't grow = a leaky bucket. Sai pours water in the top (views, followers, leads) and it pours out the holes (weak hook, no reason to follow, no call to action, no follow-up). The fix isn't more water — it's patching the holes. Each hole gets a label as he plugs it.

Topics (Sai fills): the 3–4 "holes" to label — map to his real take (e.g. hook / retention / follow / CTA, or leads / follow-up / onboarding / retention for the business version).

Verbal hook:
- A: Let me show you why your content isn't growing — with this bucket.
- B: You don't have a views problem. You have a leak. Watch.
- C: Everyone tries to pour in more. The problem is down here.

Caption hook:
- A: WHY YOUR CONTENT ISN'T GROWING
- B: YOU DON'T HAVE A VIEWS PROBLEM
- C: STOP POURING MORE IN

Visual hook:
- A: Cold open on the water already leaking out of a bucket — the visual is the hook.
- B: Sai pours water in, it runs straight out the holes at his feet.
- C: A clean bucket vs the leaky one, side by side.

Editor brief: pour in (the input everyone chases) → show it leaking → name and plug each hole one at a time → pour again, it holds → land: "patch the holes before you pour more in." Can be shot practical (real bucket) or the leak can be a HyperFrames graphic overlay if a real one is messy. Captions house style. 30–45s. 16:9.

## Video 12 (replacement) — Steal This Checklist

Replaces: jayhoovy "rules our agency runs on" listicle. That one was commandment-y ("here are the rules") which fights the not-the-expert voice, and it leaned on a borrowed $40M flex. This keeps a listicle shape but reframes it as a gift he personally uses, not a set of commands.

Status: Sai Review
Format: saveable card sequence — a tight, actionable checklist as on-screen cards Sai walks through. Save-bait.
Orientation: Horizontal.

Why it fits: replaces "rules you must follow" with "the checklist I run" (experiential, not authority); saveable format directly serves Sai's saveable-content directive; still list-shaped so it scratches the listicle itch without the guru tone; heavily pre-scriptable.

The pitch (pre-drafted, Sai confirms/tweaks): 5 questions I ask before posting any video —
1. Is there a reason to keep watching in the first 2 seconds?
2. Is it about one idea, not five?
3. Is it obvious who it's for?
4. Is there a reason to watch to the end?
5. Is there a reason to send it to someone?
(Sai can swap this checklist for a business one — e.g. before taking a new client, before hiring — same format.)

Verbal hook:
- A: Before I post anything, I run it through five questions. Steal them.
- B: This checklist is the only reason any of my videos work. It's yours.
- C: Save this — it's the five-second test I run on every video before it goes out.

Caption hook:
- A: STEAL THIS CHECKLIST
- B: THE 5 QUESTIONS I ASK BEFORE POSTING
- C: THE TEST EVERY VIDEO HAS TO PASS

Visual hook:
- A: A checklist card on screen, boxes checking off one by one as he talks.
- B: Sai holding a physical index card / notebook with the list, ticking each.
- C: Each question slams in as its own full-screen card, then a check.

Editor brief: hook (steal this) → each checklist item as a card + one line of why + a check → recap the full list on one card at the end (the freeze-frame people screenshot). Captions house style. 30–45s. 16:9. Cards built in HyperFrames, brand orange.
