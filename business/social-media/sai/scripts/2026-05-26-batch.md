## Voice Rules Applied to Every Script

From `memory/feedback-sai-linkedin-voice.md` + observed in voice memos:

- "Founder" never "CEO"
- No "most founders…" punching down — attack ideas, not peers
- No AI-flavored summary closers
- No invented specifics — every number/detail is from Sai's actual words
- Keep Sai's cuss words where he used them naturally (his voice memos cuss; his LinkedIn finals don't — flag for Sai to choose per-post)
- End on punchline, not synthesis
- Sai's pronouns: "I" and "you" — direct address, no "we" generalizations

**Drafting approach (v2 — 2026-05-26 revision):** Each script is built from Sai's own words in the voice memo, with ONLY the following changes:
- Gray's interview questions cut
- Pure filler trimmed ("um", false starts, repeated words)
- Some sentences split for readable line breaks

**What is NOT changed:** Sai's connectors ("I was like", "what I used to do is", "the way I think about it", "so"), his repetition where he repeated, his casual phrasing, his run-on energy. These are voice markers — removing them is what made the v1 drafts sound AI-flavored.

**Sources NOT consulted:** LinkedIn finals and newsletter corpus. Pulling from those was the source of AI-flavored polish in v1. For this workflow going forward, voice memo transcripts are the only voice reference.

### 1 — The 30% Taxes Vault (IN-LANE: finance)

- **Backlog entry:** `taxes-vault-30-percent`
- **Source:** Part 1 voice memo [14:24–15:32]
- **Format:** Tactic reveal — save-bait
- **Length target:** ~40s
- **Visual:** A-roll, sitting. Could overlay big text "30% → VAULT" on screen.

> This is the single greatest hack I've made in my life this year.
>
> If you hate seeing a tax bill pop up at the end of the year — listen.
>
> Every single month, take 30% of your paycheck.
>
> Open a separate vault in your bank account. Name it Taxes.
>
> Automatically deposit that 30% into the vault.
>
> That's it.
>
> End of the year hits, you don't lose a third of your bank account overnight. You're prepared. You know exactly how much you owe.
>
> And you can actually be smarter with your investments throughout the year — because that money was never available to spend in the first place.
>
> I don't have to worry about taxes anymore. I know what's going to happen.
>
> Single greatest hack I've ever made.
>
> Try it this month.

### 2 — The Plan/Work Flip (IN-LANE: discipline-schedule)

- **Backlog entry:** `plan-work-flip-60-40`
- **Source:** Part 1 voice memo [00:28–02:55]
- **Length target:** ~55s
- **Visual:** A-roll, sitting or walking. The "fourteen hours" math could be overlaid.

> I used to work for 90% of the time and plan my work for 10% of the time.
>
> I've switched to 60% planning and 40% work.
>
> And I've made more progress in my career than ever before.
>
> What I used to do is, I would just work on whatever I thought needed my attention throughout the day.
>
> Now what I do is, I think and anticipate what needs my attention over the entire week. I map everything out.
>
> Then once I go into my work sessions, I'm not thinking about what to work on. I know exactly what to work on.
>
> The way I think about it — every entrepreneur only gets 24 hours a day. Let's say you sleep for eight of those. You're left with 16. Take two hours for whatever else, like eating, talking, whatever.
>
> That leaves about 14 hours a day of undivided work.
>
> If I want as much leverage on that work as possible, there should be no thinking in those hours.
>
> Thinking should be blocked out for other times where I'm fully focused on thinking.
>
> And when I'm working, I'm fully focused on execution.

### 3 — Three Buckets / People Are the AI Moat (IN-LANE: customer-obsession)

- **Backlog entry:** `three-buckets-people-moat`
- **Source:** Part 1 voice memo [04:57–07:30]
- **Length target:** ~60s
- **Visual:** A-roll. Could overlay the three buckets on screen as Sai names them.

> If you don't know what to focus your time on, focus on improving people's experience.
>
> People are what run business.
>
> If you're ever struggling to figure out what to spend your attention on, break it into three different buckets.
>
> You have your customers. You have your team. And you have your shareholders, or your co-founding team.
>
> For your customers, it's making a really good product and a really good experience for them.
>
> For your team, it's making sure everyone has a really good time working at your company. The culture's off the charts. Everyone shows up with a sense of purpose to work every day.
>
> And for your shareholders or your co-founding team, it's about maximizing revenue, increasing profits, and giving them some sense of grounding in the midst of chaos.
>
> Something that's helped me a lot recently is thinking in the frame of these three things.
>
> And what I've found is, if you focus on the people you employ more than anything — the other two end up taking care of themselves.
>
> In the age of AI, the people who really have moats are the people who focus more than anyone else on their people.

### 4 — Document Everything / The SOP Cycle (IN-LANE: scaling)

- **Backlog entry:** `document-everything-sop-cycle`
- **Source:** Part 2 voice memo [03:35–04:55]
- **Length target:** ~55s
- **Visual:** A-roll. The 3-step cycle could be overlaid as Sai walks through it.

> My first hire failed because I had no proper systems.
>
> Without proper systems, you won't be able to scale.
>
> Everything you do as a founder needs to be documented. Everything from how you sign clients, to how you deliver your service or product.
>
> Unless that's documented, you're not going to be able to bring on people to work for you.
>
> And even if you do bring on people — they have no process for how you did it well.
>
> So if you truly want to build something that scales, you need to document everything. Step by step how to do it.
>
> And when you bring someone on, walk them through it. You do it for them. They shadow you doing it.
>
> Then you have them do it. You're there for advice while they're doing it.
>
> Then they do it on their own.
>
> And once they're doing it on their own, it should be to a point where it's as good as how you used to do it by yourself. If not better.

> So I learned the lesson — I had to fucking apply it.

> You want to know why I scaled too early?

> I cared too much about my own needs versus other people's needs.
>
> I wanted to be the cool guy making money.
