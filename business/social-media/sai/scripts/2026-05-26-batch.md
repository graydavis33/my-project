### 1 — The 30% Taxes Vault

> This is the single greatest hack I've made in my life this year.
>
> If you hate seeing a tax bill pop up at the end of the year — listen.
>
> Every single month, take 30% of your paycheck.
>
> Open a separate vault in your bank account. Name it Taxes.
>
> Automatically deposit that 30% into the vault.
>
> **That's it.**
>
> End of the year hits, you don't lose a third of your bank account overnight. You're prepared. You know exactly how much you owe.
>
> And you can actually be smarter with your investments throughout the year — because that money was never available to spend in the first place.
>
> I don't have to worry about taxes anymore. I know what's going to happen.
>
> Single greatest hack I've ever made.
>
> Try it this month.

### 2 — The Plan/Work Flip

**Hook options (pick one):**

A. "I flipped my whole workday to 60% planning and 40% work. And I've made more progress in my career than ever before."

B. "60/40. That's the ratio that changed my entire career."

C. "I used to work all day and wonder why I wasn't getting anywhere. Then I switched it."

**Script:**

> I used to spend 90% of my time working and 10% planning.
>
> I flipped it. **60% planning. 40% work.**
>
> And I've made more progress in my career than ever before.
>
> What I used to do? Just work on whatever felt urgent that day.
>
> Now I think and anticipate what needs my attention over the entire week. I map it out.
>
> Then when I sit down to work, I'm not thinking about what to do. **I already know.**
>
> The way I think about it — every founder gets 24 hours a day. Sleep eight. Two more for eating, talking, whatever.
>
> That leaves about **14 hours of undivided work.**
>
> If I want max leverage on those 14 hours — there should be no thinking in them.
>
> Thinking gets blocked out. So when I'm working, I'm just executing.

**What I'd add:**

- One concrete detail of HOW you plan — whiteboard, Notion, Sunday night session? Right now "I map it out" stays vague. One specific gives viewers something to copy.
- A closer that lands the viewer benefit. Try: "And the work itself is way better. Because I'm not deciding while I do it."

### 3 — Three Buckets / People Are the AI Moat

**Hook options (pick one):**

A. "In the age of AI, the only moat left is your people."

B. "Three buckets. That's how I decide what to focus on every single day."

C. "If you don't know what to focus on today — I have a framework for you."

**Script:**

> If you don't know what to focus on today, focus on improving people's experience.
>
> People are what run business.
>
> **Three buckets.** Your customers. Your team. Your shareholders or co-founders.
>
> For your customers — make a really good product. A really good experience.
>
> For your team — make sure everyone has a really good time working at your company. Culture's off the charts. Everyone shows up with a sense of purpose.
>
> For your shareholders — maximize revenue, increase profits, give them grounding in the middle of chaos.
>
> When you're stuck on what to focus on — pick the bucket that needs the most attention.
>
> **But here's the thing.**
>
> If you focus on the people you employ more than anything — the other two end up taking care of themselves.
>
> **In the age of AI, the people who have moats are the ones who focus more than anyone else on their people.**

**What I'd add:**

- The "in the age of AI" line is your best line but it's at the end. If a viewer drops at 30s they miss it. Hook A pulls it up front — if you go with A, end on the bucket framework instead.
- A concrete example for the "team" bucket. Without it, "everyone has a really good time" stays abstract. If you have a specific Trendify tactic you actually use, drop it in for save-bait.

### 4 — Document Everything / The SOP Cycle

**Hook options (pick one):**

A. "My first hire failed. Because I had no systems."

B. "Zero. That's how many systems I had when I made my first hire. And he failed."

C. "If I had to start a business over, I'd document every single thing I do — before hiring anyone."

**Script:**

> My first hire failed. **Because I had no systems.**
>
> Without proper systems, you can't scale.
>
> Everything you do as a founder needs to be documented. How you sign clients. How you deliver your service. How you build the product. **Every step.**
>
> Because if it's not documented, you can't bring on people to work for you. And even if you do — they have no idea how you actually did it well.
>
> So you have to document everything. Step by step.
>
> Then when you bring someone on, walk them through it.
>
> **One.** You do it. They shadow you.
>
> **Two.** They do it. You're there for advice.
>
> **Three.** They do it on their own.
>
> And once they're doing it on their own — it should be as good as how you used to do it. If not better.

**What I'd add:**

- Examples of what to document. Quick beat like "every client onboarding call. Every editing decision. Every Slack you send a client." Makes the abstract concrete and screenshot-able.
- A sharper closer. The ending lands flat. Try: "If they can't do it without you — you didn't build a system. You built a habit." (My line, not yours — only use if it lands for you.)

### 5 — When to Scale Up / Gods of Business

**Hook options (pick one):**

A. "I scaled my business too early. And got slapped in the face by the gods of business."

B. "Five years. That's how long it took me to learn when to scale a business."

C. "Here's the signal that it's time to scale your business."

**Script:**

> Two things have to be true before you scale.
>
> **One** — your customers are truly happy. They keep buying every single month.
>
> **Two** — if you have a team, your team is happy. They're retaining. They show up to work with excitement.
>
> **That's the signal.**
>
> It took me five years to figure that out.
>
> I scaled without having those two things be true.
>
> **And I got slapped in the face by the gods of business.**
>
> The business didn't work out. Customers were unhappy. My team was unhappy.
>
> So I learned the lesson — I had to fucking apply it.
>
> You want to know why I scaled too early?
>
> I cared too much about my own needs versus other people's.
>
> **I wanted to be the cool guy making money.**

*Alt close if dropping the cuss: "So I learned the lesson — and I had to apply it the hard way."*

**What I'd add:**

- Nothing structural — this is the strongest script in the batch. The self-incrimination at the end is gold.
- If you pick Hook A (failure-first), add a 1-sentence bridge before "Two things have to be true" — e.g., "I had to learn that the hard way." So the framework lands cleanly after the cold open.

### 6 — Mentor / Basketball Coach

**Hook options (pick one):**

A. "My 8th grade basketball coach is the reason I'm running a business at 21."

B. "Everything I've built — I can backtrack to one person who believed in me when I didn't."

C. "Before you find a single customer or a single hire, find one person who believes in you."

**Script:**

> Find someone who's done what you want to do.
>
> And have them believe in you before you even believe in yourself.
>
> My 8th grade basketball coach was also my 7th grade coach.
>
> From 6th grade, he believed in me. Had me sign up for every weightlifting class. Every intramural game.
>
> **And in 7th grade — he cut me from the team. Because I wasn't good enough.**
>
> But he told me to keep trying. That he saw something in me.
>
> And I didn't even see that in myself.
>
> **8th grade — I made the team.**
>
> I don't know if I would have made it without him.
>
> It's not that this coach was a million times better at basketball than me.
>
> **It's that he believed in me — when I didn't even believe in myself.**
>
> Find that person.
>
> Not for advice. For knowing what's possible.

**What I'd add:**

- A bridge from the basketball story to business application. Viewers think "great story but I'm not in 8th grade." Try: "I still apply this. The reason I built what I built is because someone believed in me before I did."
- Old photos / yearbook shots from that era overlaid when you say "8th grade" — huge emotional weight if any exist.

### 7 — Backtracking Fear / The Ads Story

**Hook options (pick one):**

A. "Ten thousand dollars a month. That's what I was spending on ads in 2024 — until they stopped working."

B. "I haven't run a single ad on my business in two years. Because I was scared."

C. "When you're afraid of something — you can usually backtrack it to one moment."

**Script:**

> When you're afraid of something, you can usually backtrack it to one moment.
>
> It's actually a really helpful exercise.
>
> In 2024, I was spending close to **ten thousand dollars a month** on ads to grow my business.
>
> We were signing clients. Then the ads fatigued. They stopped working.
>
> **And I was like — I can't do this anymore.**
>
> That gave me fear of running ads ever again.
>
> So for the next two years, I didn't.
>
> Instead I used that fear to go inward. Rebuilt the product. Rebuilt the service. Made something I'm actually proud of.
>
> Now we're getting ready to run ads again — for the first time since 2024.
>
> **And I find myself scared.**
>
> Not because we're running ads.
>
> **Because of what happened the time before.**
>
> The second I traced the fear back to that — it kind of goes away.
>
> Whatever you're afraid of right now — trace it back. **The fear is almost never about the thing in front of you.**

**What I'd add:**

- I restored the "trace it back" closer because the v2 ending faded. This gives viewers a takeaway they can act on. Cut it if it doesn't feel like you.
- Optional: end on present-tense stakes — "I have no idea if these new ads will work. But I'm doing them anyway." Real and raw — makes the lesson stick.
