## The 7 Scripts

### 1 — The 30% Taxes Vault (IN-LANE: finance)

- **Backlog entry:** `taxes-vault-30-percent`
- **Source:** Part 1 voice memo [14:24–15:32]
- **Format:** Tactic reveal — save-bait
- **Length target:** ~40s
- **Visual:** A-roll, sitting. Could overlay big text "30% → VAULT" on screen.

> This is the single greatest hack I've made in my life this year.
>
> If you hate seeing a tax bill pop up at the end of the year — listen.
>
> Every single month, take 30% of your paycheck.
>
> Open a separate vault in your bank account. Name it Taxes.
>
> Automatically deposit that 30% into the vault.
>
> That's it.
>
> End of the year hits, you don't lose a third of your bank account overnight. You're prepared. You know exactly how much you owe.
>
> And you can actually be smarter with your investments throughout the year — because that money was never available to spend in the first place.
>
> I don't have to worry about taxes anymore. I know what's going to happen.
>
> Single greatest hack I've ever made.
>
> Try it this month.

### 2 — The Plan/Work Flip (IN-LANE: discipline-schedule)

- **Backlog entry:** `plan-work-flip-60-40`
- **Source:** Part 1 voice memo [00:28–02:55]
- **Format:** Hot Take confession + tactical math
- **Length target:** ~50s
- **Visual:** A-roll, sitting OR walking. The "fourteen hours" math could be overlaid.

> I used to spend 90% of my time working, and 10% planning.
>
> I flipped it. 60% planning, 40% work.
>
> I've made more progress in my career than I ever had before.
>
> Here's the math.
>
> Every entrepreneur gets 24 hours a day. Sleep eight. That leaves sixteen. Two for whatever — eating, talking.
>
> So you've got fourteen hours of undivided work.
>
> If I want max leverage on those fourteen hours, there should be no thinking in them.
>
> Thinking gets its own block.
>
> Working gets its own block.
>
> Now when I sit down to work, I'm not figuring out what to do. I already know.
>
> I just execute.
>
> I respect my time more. People respect my time more. And productivity across the entire business has gone up.

### 3 — Three Buckets / People Are the AI Moat (IN-LANE: customer-obsession)

- **Backlog entry:** `three-buckets-people-moat`
- **Source:** Part 1 voice memo [04:57–07:30]
- **Format:** Framework + Hot Take closer
- **Length target:** ~55s
- **Visual:** A-roll. Could overlay the three buckets on screen as Sai names them.

> If you don't know what to focus your time on, focus on improving people's experience.
>
> People are what run business.
>
> Three buckets.
>
> Customers. Make a really good product. Make a great experience.
>
> Your team. Make sure everyone has a really good time working at your company. Culture off the charts. Everyone shows up with purpose.
>
> Your shareholders or your co-founders. Maximize revenue. Increase profits. Give them grounding in the middle of chaos.
>
> When you're stuck on what to focus on, pick the bucket that needs the most attention.
>
> But here's the thing.
>
> If you focus on the people you employ more than anything — the other two end up taking care of themselves.
>
> In the age of AI, the people who have moats are the people who focus more than anyone else on their people.

### 4 — Document Everything / The SOP Cycle (IN-LANE: scaling)

- **Backlog entry:** `document-everything-sop-cycle`
- **Source:** Part 2 voice memo [03:35–04:55]
- **Format:** Framework + personal stake opener
- **Length target:** ~50s
- **Visual:** A-roll. The 3-step cycle could be overlaid as Sai says it.

> My first hire failed because I had no systems.
>
> Here's what I learned.
>
> Without proper systems, you cannot scale.
>
> Everything you do as a founder needs to be documented. How you sign clients. How you deliver your service. How you build the product.
>
> When you bring someone on, the cycle goes like this.
>
> One. You do it. They watch.
>
> Two. They do it. You're there for advice.
>
> Three. They do it on their own.
>
> By the third step, what they do should be as good as how you used to do it. If not better.
>
> Without that cycle, you'll keep doing every job yourself.
>
> And every founder who can't let go of every job — never builds anything bigger than themselves.

> But not for the reason you think.

### 7 — Backtracking Fear / The Ads Story (NEW TERRITORY: fear-mindset)

- **Backlog entry:** `backtracking-fear-ads-story`
- **Source:** Part 2 voice memo [00:04–02:00]
- **Format:** Personal story / vulnerable confession with present-tense stakes
- **Length target:** ~55s
- **Visual:** A-roll. Sitting, intimate framing — this is the vulnerable one.

> When you're afraid of something, you can usually backtrack it to something.
>
> In 2024, I was spending close to ten thousand dollars a month on paid ads to grow my business.
>
> It worked for a while. We were signing clients.
>
> Then the ads fatigued. They stopped working.
>
> That gave me fear of ever running ads again.
>
> So for the next two years, I didn't.
>
> Instead, I used that fear to go inward.
>
> I rebuilt the product. I rebuilt the service. I made something I'm actually proud of.
>
> Now we're getting ready to start running ads again — for the first time since 2024.
>
> And I find myself scared.
>
> But it's not because we're running ads.
>
> It's because of what happened the time before.
>
> Once I traced it back to that — the fear went away.
>
> Whatever you're afraid of right now — backtrack it. The fear is almost never about the thing in front of you.
