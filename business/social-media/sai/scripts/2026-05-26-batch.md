### 1 — The 30% Taxes Vault

> Every single month, take 30% of your paycheck.
>
> Open a separate vault in your bank account. Name it Taxes.
>
> Automatically deposit that 30% into the vault.
>
> End of the year hits, you don't lose a third of your bank account overnight. You're prepared.
>
> And you can actually be smarter with your investments throughout the year, because that money was never available to spend in the first place.
>
> Adopting this skill will force you to always win against taxes.
>
> Single greatest financial hack I've ever made.

### 2 — The Plan/Work Flip

A. My business changed when I went from 90/10 to 60/40, let me explain.

B. 60/40. That's the ratio that changed my entire career.

C. I used to work all day and wonder why I wasn't getting anywhere. Then I switched it.

**Script:**

> I used to spend 90% of my time working and 10% planning.
>
> Once I changed it to 60% planning. 40% work.
>
> I started making more progress in my career than ever before.
>
> I used to just work on whatever felt urgent that day.
>
> Now I think and anticipate what needs my attention over the entire week. I map it out.
>
> Then when I sit down to work, I'm not thinking about what to do. I already know.
>
> Every founder gets 24 hours a day. Sleep eight. Two more for eating, talking, whatever.
>
> That leaves about 14 hours of undivided work.
>
> If I want max leverage on those 14 hours, you need to block out time for thinking and planning.
>
> So when I'm working, I'm just executing.

**What I'd add:**

- One concrete detail of HOW you plan — whiteboard, Notion, Sunday night session? Right now "I map it out" stays vague.

### 3 — Three Buckets / People Are the AI Moat

A. In the age of AI, the only moat left are people.

B. Three buckets. That's how I decide what to focus on every single day.

C. If you don't know what to focus on today — I have a framework for you.

**Script:**

> **(A/C intro):** If you don't know what to focus on today, focus on improving people's experience.
>
> People are what run business.
>
> **(B intro):** Three buckets. Your customers. Your team. Your shareholders or co-founders.
>
> For your customers — make a really good product/experience.
>
> For your team — make sure everyone has a passion for the work. Culture's off the charts. Everyone shows up with a sense of purpose.
>
> For your shareholders — maximize revenue, increase profits, give them grounding in the middle of chaos.
>
> When you're stuck on what to focus on, pick the bucket that needs the most attention.
>
> To speedrun this framework — you should focus on the people you employ more than anything, because then the other two end up taking care of themselves.
>
> In order to have a moat in the age of AI, you must focus more than anyone else on your people.
>
> Customers. Team. Shareholders.

### 4 — Document Everything / The SOP Cycle

A. My first hire failed. Because I had no systems.

B. You can't retain employees without documenting, here's why.

C. If I had to start a business over, I'd document every single thing I do, before hiring anyone.

**Script:**

> Without proper systems, you can't scale.
>
> Everything you do as a founder needs to be documented. How you sign clients. How you deliver your service. How you build the product. Every step.
>
> Because if it's not documented, you can't bring on people to work for you. And even if you do, they have no idea how you actually did it well.
>
> So, document each step.
>
> To avoid the mistakes I made with my first hire, here is the stupidly simple process I use for new employees.
>
> One. You do it. They shadow you.
>
> Two. They do it. You're there for advice.
>
> Three. They do it on their own.
>
> If they can't do it without you, you didn't build a system. You built a habit.

**What I'd add:**

- Examples of what to document. Quick beat like: every client onboarding call, every editing decision, every Slack you send a client. Makes the abstract concrete.

### 7 — Backtracking Fear / The Ads Story

A. Ten thousand dollars a month. That's what I was spending on ads in 2024, until they stopped working.

B. I haven't run a single ad for my business in two years. Because I was scared.

C. When you're afraid of something, you can usually trace it back to one moment.

**Script:**

> **(A/B intro):** When you're afraid of something, you can usually trace it back to one moment.
>
> **(C intro):** In 2024, I was spending close to ten thousand dollars a month on ads to grow my business.
>
> We were signing clients. Then the ads fatigued. They stopped working.
>
> And they stopped working because my product was honestly not great.
>
> The product wasn't bad, but I felt guilty for the money people were spending on it. ⚠️ (Sai flagged: "correct this if wrong, I feel like I remember you saying this yesterday")
>
> That gave me fear of running ads ever again.
>
> So for the next two years, I didn't.
>
> Instead, I used that fear to go inward. Rebuilt the product. Rebuilt the service. Made something I'm actually proud of.
>
> Now we're getting ready to run ads again, for the first time since 2024.
>
> And I find myself scared.
>
> Not because we're running ads.
>
> Because of what happened the time before.
>
> The second I traced the fear back to that, it kind of went away.
>
> Whatever you're afraid of right now, trace it back. The fear is almost never about the thing in front of you.
