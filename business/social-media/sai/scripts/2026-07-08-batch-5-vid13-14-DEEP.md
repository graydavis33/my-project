# VIDEO 13 — The 1% Entrepreneur

Format: myth-buster scoreboard. Each beat crosses out a talent/vanity metric and swaps in a controllable INPUT metric with a real number. Thesis: the top 1% isn't about talent you're born with — it's about inputs anyone can choose.

Length: 45–70s. Beats marked CORE are the spine; beats marked TRIM-OK can be cut if it runs long.

Orientation: Horizontal.

Setup: Sai talking head + a scoreboard/whiteboard he can gesture to. The scoreboard graphic (two columns: WHAT WE THINK vs WHAT ACTUALLY PREDICTS IT) is built in HyperFrames as an overlay Gray keys in — cards cross out and swap on Sai's beat.

-------------------------------------------------------------------

## The spoken script (Sai, word for word)

COLD OPEN (0–3s) — challenge the assumption. CORE.

Sai: We all think the top one percent of founders are just born different. Smarter. More talented. I went and looked at what the research actually says — and it's kind of the opposite.

On screen: THE 1% AREN'T WHO YOU THINK
Visual: Sai turns to a scoreboard with two blank columns.

-------------------------------------------------------------------

BEAT 1 — discipline beats IQ. CORE. (This is the anchor — keep it first.)

Sai: Researchers at Penn tracked students for a full year. Plain self-discipline — just doing the boring thing consistently — predicted who succeeded more than twice as well as IQ did. Not talent. Discipline.

On screen: IQ  ✗  →  SELF-DISCIPLINE  ✓
Number card: 2x better predictor than IQ
Citation: Duckworth & Seligman, 2005

-------------------------------------------------------------------

BEAT 2 — grit at West Point. TRIM-OK.

Sai: They ran a version of this on the hardest training in the U.S. military. What predicted who made it through wasn't fitness or test scores — it was grit. How long you keep going. It beat every talent score they had.

On screen: TALENT SCORES  ✗  →  GRIT  ✓
Number card: out-predicted every aptitude test for who finishes
Citation: Duckworth, West Point study, 2007

-------------------------------------------------------------------

BEAT 3 — age / experience. CORE. (Best myth-buster — lands hard.)

Sai: And that young-genius-in-a-hoodie picture? The average founder of the fastest-growing startups — the top point-one percent — is forty-five. A fifty-year-old is almost twice as likely to build one as a thirty-year-old. Experience wins.

On screen: BEING YOUNG  ✗  →  EXPERIENCE  ✓
Number card: top 0.1% of founders average age 45
Citation: MIT + U.S. Census, 2020

-------------------------------------------------------------------

BEAT 4 — reps / no's. CORE.

Sai: Airbnb got turned down by investor after investor — the same pitch that's worth billions now. Venture funds pass on about ninety-nine percent of what they see. The founders who make it aren't rejection-proof. They just took more swings.

On screen: NEVER GETTING REJECTED  ✗  →  MORE REPS  ✓
Number card: Airbnb — 7 investors passed · VCs fund ~1%
Citation: Chesky, 2015 · Gompers et al., Stanford, 2020

-------------------------------------------------------------------

BEAT 5 — iterations. CORE. (Most visual / memorable.)

Sai: James Dyson built five thousand, one hundred and twenty-six vacuums that failed before one finally worked. Number five-thousand-one-hundred-twenty-seven is the one in your house.

On screen: GETTING IT RIGHT  ✗  →  ITERATIONS  ✓
Number card: 5,126 failed prototypes
Citation: James Dyson (his own count)

-------------------------------------------------------------------

BEAT 6 — learning input / books. CORE. (Gray specifically asked for this one.)

Sai: And the boring one nobody posts about — learning. A Yale study found people who read every day literally stay sharper for life, even after you account for money and education. Buffett says he reads five hundred pages a day.

On screen: LIKES  ✗  →  LEARNING  ✓
Number card: daily readers stay sharper — even controlling for wealth & schooling
Citation: Yale / Bavishi et al., 2016 · Buffett

-------------------------------------------------------------------

LAND (the close). CORE.

Sai: None of this is talent. Hours, reps, no's, pages — those are inputs. Stuff you and I actually control. The one percent isn't the most gifted. It's the people who outlasted the boring numbers. So the real question is which number are you actually willing to hit.

On screen: IT'S INPUT, NOT TALENT
Visual: the whole scoreboard fills — vanity column all crossed out, input column glowing with its numbers.

# VIDEO 14 — Business Trivia

Format: quiz. Gray asks the question, Sai answers. Question opens a curiosity loop, the answer closes it. Four options A/B/C/D, a beat to guess, then reveal + a one-line "why it matters." Number of questions = the finish-line counter in the corner. Reusable engine.

Length: 30–60s (5 questions, keep each tight). Horizontal.

Directing note: give Sai the answers and reveal lines ahead of time. He can either answer confidently or take a real guess first and then explain — either plays. Gray reads the question and the four options.

-------------------------------------------------------------------

## Question 1 — the higher price that sold more

GRAY: Same exact dress, two different prices. Which one sold more?
A) $34
B) $39
C) They sold the same
D) $44

Correct: B ($39)

SAI: The thirty-nine dollar one — by about a third more. Higher price, more sales. Prices ending in nine just signal "deal" to our brains. Pricing is psychology, not math.

On-screen citation: MIT & University of Chicago field experiment, 2003

-------------------------------------------------------------------

## Question 2 — the decoy that sold the expensive option

GRAY: The Economist offered three plans — web only for fifty-nine, print only for one-twenty-five, and print-plus-web also for one-twenty-five. Almost nobody picked print-only. So why did they keep it?
A) A mistake
B) It hurt sales
C) It pushed the expensive bundle from 32% to 84% of buyers
D) It made people buy nothing

Correct: C

SAI: That "useless" middle option is doing all the work. With it there, eighty-four percent bought the top bundle. Take it away and it drops to thirty-two. A decoy nobody chooses makes your best option look like a steal.

On-screen citation: Dan Ariely, MIT, Predictably Irrational, 2008

-------------------------------------------------------------------

## Question 3 — how fast you're judged

GRAY: How long does it take a stranger to decide whether they trust your face?
A) A tenth of a second
B) 7 seconds
C) 30 seconds
D) 2 minutes

Correct: A

SAI: A tenth of a second. One hundred milliseconds. Give people more time and they don't change their mind — they just get more confident in it. Your thumbnail and your first frame get judged before you say a single word.

On-screen citation: Princeton (Willis & Todorov), 2006

-------------------------------------------------------------------

## Question 4 — why startups actually fail

GRAY: What's the number one reason startups fail?
A) Ran out of money
B) Got beaten by a competitor
C) Built something nobody needed
D) The team fell apart

Correct: C

SAI: No market need — about forty-two percent. Running out of money is usually just the symptom. They built something nobody actually wanted. Same rule for content: make what people already want, don't guess.

On-screen citation: CB Insights, 2014

-------------------------------------------------------------------

## Question 5 — the checkout leak

GRAY: Out of ten people who put something in an online cart, how many leave without buying?
A) About 3
B) About 5
C) About 7
D) About 9

Correct: C

SAI: About seven — seventy percent walk. And the number one reason is surprise fees at checkout. The sale isn't lost on the price, it's lost on the shipping cost you spring at the end.

On-screen citation: Baymard Institute (average of 49 studies)

-------------------------------------------------------------------

## Bonus questions (swap-ins if Sai wants to change one)

First offer wins — GRAY: In a price negotiation, who usually gets the better deal — the one who names a number first, or the one who waits? Correct: names first (anchoring). SAI: Naming the first number anchors the whole thing around you. Don't wait for the client to say their budget — put your number down first. Source: Galinsky & Mussweiler, 2001.

One second of an ad — GRAY: How long does a video ad need to be seen to lift brand recall? A) under 1 second B) 5s C) 10s D) the full ad. Correct: A. SAI: Under a second. Even views under three seconds lifted ad recall almost fifty percent. Front-load your logo and your hook. Source: Facebook / Nielsen, 2015.

The real habit timeline — GRAY: The famous rule says 21 days to build a habit. What did the actual study find? A) 21 days B) 66 days on average, range 18 to 254 C) 30 days D) 90 days. Correct: B. SAI: Sixty-six days on average, and it ranges wildly — up to two hundred fifty. Twenty-one days is a myth. Quitting at three weeks is quitting way too early. Source: Lally et al., 2010.
