The Vault, Episode 1
Team One-on-One Questions

How to use: ask in this order, safest to hottest. The belief echo goes last so the earlier answers stay unprimed. Before the one-on-ones, film Sai predicting each person's answers. Every answer then cuts as prediction vs reality, which is the Bezos dashboard mechanic made visual.


Question 1
When's the last time you bit your tongue in a meeting here?

Follow up: What was it?

Why it works: everyone has one, and the viewer immediately wonders if they will actually say it on camera. It proves the filter between Sai and the team exists through a real story instead of asking about honesty in the abstract.


Question 2
If you ran Trendify tomorrow, what's the first thing you'd change?

Follow up: Could we fix that by Friday if I just said yes?

Why it works: role reversal forces a specific answer, and the viewer waits to see if it's cosmetic or structural. The follow up turns the answer into a shippable change that feeds the success bar.


Question 3
What do I do that makes your job harder?

Follow up: When's the last time a decision got made without asking the person doing the work?

Why it works: an employee critiquing the boss to his face is the exact tension the episode promised. The pause before the answer is the most watchable silence in the video. The follow up escalates from Sai's habits to the company's habits.


Question 4
When's the last time you thought about leaving?

Follow up: What kept you?

Why it works: highest personal stakes on the list. Everyone has an answer and the viewer knows it. The what kept you follow up usually lands somewhere warm, so the beat resolves human, not brutal.


Question 5, the belief echo
Play them the clip of Sai stating his belief (everyone here wants this as much as I do) and film their face while they watch. Then ask:

How true is that?

Follow up: silence. Let them fill it.

Why it works: this is the episode's master question said out loud. The reaction shot while the clip plays is the most watchable moment available to this episode. It must go last, because asked any earlier it primes every other answer.


The standing closer, every conversation
Last one. Name one thing slowing you down that we could fix by Friday, if I said yes right now.

Five conversations means five yes moments, and the ship three changes checklist fills itself on camera.


The setup, these are remote (Zoom), not in person

The 5 conversations happen over Zoom. That's not a compromise to hide. The "founder interrogating his team over a video call" look is the texture, so lean into it. Two realities shape every shot:

Sai's side is ours to control: real camera, real mic, real light, in the room. Broadcast quality.
The employee's side is theirs: a webcam feed we can only partly influence. Treat it as documentary footage, not a controlled shot. The gap between Sai's clean image and their raw webcam is the intimacy.


The shots, the Zoom one-on-one setup (repeat for each of the 5 conversations)

The capture, every source rolling at once so the edit has options:

[ ] Zoom recording. Turn on "record a separate audio and video file for each participant" (cloud recording) so the employee's face and Sai's face are separate clean layers, not baked into one gallery grid. This is the spine of the beat.
[ ] Sai's real camera. A proper cam on Sai at his setup, framed like his A-roll. This is the listener cam. His face hearing the question 3 and 4 answers is half the beat, and it's the only broadcast-quality face in the call.
[ ] Sai's clean mic. Lav or shotgun on Sai, recorded locally, not through Zoom, so his side is crisp. Zoom audio is the employee's-side backup.
[ ] The over-the-shoulder. A wide of Sai in the room with the employee live on his monitor. The shot that sells "this is a real remote call," and the go-to reset frame between questions.
[ ] Screen record of Sai's monitor. The actual Zoom window. Lets you punch to full-screen employee, show the "two faces on a call" framing, or use the interface itself as a visual.

The employee's side, a 20-second ask before the call (send it ahead):

[ ] Good light and face the window, headphones or earbuds for clean audio, strongest wifi, laptop propped at eye level. A one-line message. Their webcam is what it is, but lit and stable beats dark and floaty.

Question 5, the belief echo. The must-get of the whole episode:

[ ] Sai screen-shares the belief clip into the Zoom. The employee watches it inside the call.
[ ] Their reaction lands on the separate-participant recording. This is the frame you protect above everything. The whole episode points at it.
[ ] Hold Sai's listener cam too, him watching them watch it.
[ ] Hold through the silence after "how true is that?" No cuts, no re-asks.

Around each call (filmed on Sai's real camera, in the room):

[ ] Sai reset. 20 to 30 seconds to camera right after hanging up, what just landed. Feeds the rehook and confessional material.
[ ] The dial-in or the hang-up. Sai starting or ending the call. Transition tissue, and the nerves live here.
[ ] Timestamp log. The second anything sounds like a future change, write down the clip and time. This feeds the takeaways triage.


The editing style, fluid, emotion sets the pace

No fixed cutting rhythm for this section. The pace floats with the room's temperature and is allowed to swing hard, intense, serious, funny, sad, back to light, inside the same beat. Let the moment dictate. Don't impose a grid.

Heavy, serious, sad: let it breathe. Long uncut holds, single face, no cover cuts. Punch in slow on the raw webcam. The low-fi image reads more intimate the tighter you go. Silence is a cut you don't make.

Light, funny, banter: snap it up. Quick back-and-forth between Sai's cam and the employee's feed, jump cuts fine, ride the energy.

The whiplash is the point: a laugh cut hard into a gut-punch answer hits harder than either alone. Build at least a couple of those turns on purpose.

The two textures are a tool: cut to Sai's clean broadcast face for weight and composure, stay on the raw webcam for honesty and nerves. Bounce between them to steer how a moment feels.

The reset frame: the over-the-shoulder "two people on a call" wide is your exhale between emotional swings and your re-establish after a long punch-in.

Score floats with it, and sometimes drops out: kill the music entirely under the heaviest answers. The silence and webcam hiss is the sound of the truth landing. Bring it back for the lighter runs.

Prediction vs reality: Sai's pre-filmed prediction cuts in against the real answer wherever the gap is sharp. It's the recurring device, not a one-time gag.


Parked for the implementation beat (not interview questions)
