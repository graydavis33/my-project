There is **no documented figure** for how much time Bezos spends with customers — no source says "2 hours a week" or any weekly amount. Do **not** state a number in the video. His customer contact is a set of **habits**, below.

---

## The real tactics (use these instead of a number)

**1. The "?" email.** Bezos keeps a public address, **jeff@amazon.com**, reads the customer emails himself, and forwards complaints to the responsible executive with a single character: **"?"**.
> *"I see most of those emails. I forward them to the executives in charge of the area with a question mark… It's shorthand. 'Can you look into this? Why is this happening?'"* — Jeff Bezos, 2018 Bush Center interview (CNBC, Inc, Yahoo Finance)

**2. The empty chair.** Bezos left an empty seat at the conference table to represent the customer — *"the most important person in the room."* (Inc, Entrepreneur, Benzinga)

**3. Work backwards.** Every Amazon project starts with a mock press release + FAQ written from the customer's point of view, before anything is built. — Amazon Leadership Principles (amazon.jobs)

**4. Believe the customer over the dashboard.**
> *"When the data and the anecdotes disagree, the anecdotes are usually right."* — Jeff Bezos, 2016 shareholder letter (aboutamazon.com)

## The story for the video ⭐
In a meeting, Amazon's head of customer service insisted phone wait times were short. Bezos said *"Okay, let's call,"* dialed Amazon's **1-800 customer service line live in the room, and everyone sat in silence for 10+ minutes** before anyone picked up — proving the metric was wrong. (Bezos has recounted this; startuparchive, UNILAD, HR Grapevine)

---

## Why he does it (his words)
- **Customer Obsession is Amazon's #1 Leadership Principle:** *"Leaders start with the customer and work backwards… Although leaders pay attention to competitors, they obsess over customers."* (amazon.jobs)
- *"Obsessive customer focus is by far the most protective of Day 1 vitality."* — 2016 letter
- He calls customers **"divinely discontent"** — their expectations never stop rising, which is what forces the company to keep improving. — 2017 letter
