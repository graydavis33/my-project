The "how Bezos actually did it" segment. **Sai narrates this near the top**, right after the draw, to give viewers full context without losing them. Built to teach the whole tactic through one story, then pull straight into Sai's twist.

- **Placement:** Beat 4 in `VAULT-EP1-STORY-OUTLINE.md` (after THE DRAW, before THE TWIST).
- **Runtime:** ~50–65 sec at natural pace.
- **Voice:** Sai — honest, direct, a little amused. Talking *to* the viewer, not reciting facts.
- **No time figures.** "2 hours a week" is unsourced — it's banned. The tactic is the *habit*, not a number (see `VAULT-EP1-BEZOS-BACKGROUND.md`).

## FULL READ VERSION (context + implementation — plain text, read off this)

Let me tell you something about Jeff Bezos that most people don't know.

Early Amazon. He's in a meeting, and his head of customer service is sitting there swearing up and down that customers wait less than a minute to get help on the phone. The data says so. It's right there on the screen — green check mark, everybody's happy.

And Bezos just goes — okay. Let's call.

He picks up the phone, dials his own customer service line, right there in the middle of the meeting, puts it on speaker... and the whole room just sits there. One minute. Five minutes. Over ten minutes of silence before a single person picks up.

Ten minutes of grown adults staring at a phone — proving the number on the screen was a complete lie.

And that's the tactic I have to steal. Bezos refused to run his company off of reports. His rule was simple: when the data says one thing and the real stories say another, the stories are right. So he went straight to the source and checked for himself.

It's the same reason he kept his personal email public and read the complaints himself. It's why he left one empty chair in every meeting — for the customer — the most important person in the room, who's never actually in the room. The guy built one of the biggest companies on earth by refusing to believe his own dashboard.

Now here's where it gets uncomfortable for me.

Bezos did all of this for his customers. But my most important "customers" right now aren't the people buying from me — they're the five people who show up every day and build this thing with me. My team.

And if I'm being honest, I've got a whole dashboard in my head about them. I assume they're aligned. I assume they're happy with how we do things. I assume I know what's working.

So I'm going to do exactly what Bezos did. I'm going to stop assuming — and go check.

I'm sitting down with all five of them, one on one, and instead of talking, I'm going to shut up and listen. No fixing, no defending. I just want to hear what they actually think — even the stuff I don't want to hear.

And then the hard part: whatever they tell me, I'm pulling a real list of changes and shipping at least three of them before this video is over.

Because anybody can sit and listen and nod. The whole point is what you do after.

So let's find out what I've been getting wrong.

## WHY THIS KEEPS THEM WATCHING

- **Opens on a story, not a fact** — the 10-minute silence is a built-in cliffhanger; the viewer needs to know how it ends before they'll click away.
- **Teaches the whole tactic through one scene** — they *understand* "trust reality over the dashboard" because they watched it happen, not because they were told.
- **Stacks the other habits fast** (email, empty chair) once they're already bought in — no list fatigue.
- **Ends on an open loop** — "the most important people aren't my customers at all" forces the next beat.
- **Zero unsourced claims** — every line traces to the verified background doc.
