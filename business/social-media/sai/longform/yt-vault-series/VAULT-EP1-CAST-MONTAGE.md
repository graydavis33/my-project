# The Vault — Ep 1: Cast Montage (Beat 7 — the 5 interviewees)

The required ingredients for the backstory montage of the 5 employees, person-agnostic — works no matter who gets picked. Duplicate the PER-PERSON block at the bottom, one copy per employee, once the 5 are chosen. For Google Docs, paste from `VAULT-EP1-CAST-MONTAGE.txt`.

**The design rule: every intro detail is a loaded gun.** A detail only earns its spot in the montage if it pays off later in one of the 5 interview questions (`VAULT-EP1-TEAM-QUESTIONS.md`). Same lean rule as the takeaways script — nothing on camera that doesn't come back.

Last updated: 2026-07-07

## The idea in one paragraph

The montage exists so the 1-on-1 answers land. The viewer needs to tell 5 people apart instantly, know each person's relationship to Sai, and hear Sai's assumption about each of them BEFORE the conversations — so every answer cuts as prediction vs reality. That's the Bezos dashboard mechanic made visual: the montage is Sai's dashboard, the interviews are the phone call.

## Context — 6 things the script must establish per person

1. **NAME + WHAT THEY ACTUALLY DO** — plain English, not the job title. "She runs every client onboarding" beats "Account Manager." One line.
2. **TENURE + HOW THEY GOT HERE** — first hire, intern who stayed, poached from somewhere. Pays off in Q4 ("when's the last time you thought about leaving?") — time invested = stakes.
3. **RELATIONSHIP TO SAI, ONE CONCRETE DETAIL** — "the first person I ever hired," "the only one who argues with me," "we talk every day and I still don't know if he's honest with me." Pays off in Q1 and Q3 (bit your tongue / what do I do that makes your job harder) — the honesty questions only have tension if the viewer knows the dynamic.
4. **WHAT THEY OWN** — the thing that breaks if they disappear tomorrow. Pays off in Q2 ("if you ran Trendify tomorrow...") — their answer carries weight because we established their domain.
5. **SAI'S ONE-LINE READ ON THEM** — his assumption, stated out loud: "X never complains." "Y is the most bought-in person here." The most load-bearing line of the six: when their 1-on-1 answer contradicts the read, this line becomes the callback — prediction vs reality.
6. **ONE HUMANIZING SPECIFIC** — a quirk, a habit, a desk detail. Makes them a character instead of a role, so the viewer can tell 5 people apart at speed.

## Film — the per-person shot kit (identical setup for all 5 so they cut as a set)

1. **HERO PORTRAIT** — same framing, same lens, same lighting for everyone. Consistency is what makes it read as a cast reveal instead of random clips.
2. **AT-WORK B-ROLL** — 2–3 clips of them doing the thing they own. Horizontal (reusable library footage).
3. **ONE TWO-SHOT WITH SAI** — working, laughing, mid-conversation. The relationship established visually, not just narrated.
4. **THE QUIRK ON FILM** — ingredient 6 captured, not described.
5. **A CLEAN FACE SINGLE** — reaction-ready. Needed later for the belief-echo playback and the announce moments; bank it in this same setup.
6. **SAI'S PREDICTION CLIP** — Sai to camera predicting this person's answers, filmed BEFORE any 1-on-1 happens. Whether it lives in the montage or right after, it must be shot at montage time — it cannot exist after the interviews.

## Production notes

- **Format:** introduce each person as a **mini vault file** — same design language as the Bezos file (photo, name, one-line role, smaller format). The vault holds files on the greats; now it opens five new ones. The commit cards already use this styling.
- **Pace:** 10–15 seconds per person, 60–75 seconds total.
- **Narration:** the spine comes from the sit-down A-roll (films last), so the montage assembles fine before the interview is ever recorded.
- **Open decision** (story outline B8, decision 4): real names on screen or not. The vault-file cards need this answered before design.
