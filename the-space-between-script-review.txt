THE SPACE BETWEEN — SCRIPT REVIEW
By Ari Goren
Reviewed: April 25, 2026
13-page short film draft


SUMMARY

The story works. The emotional spine is clear, the central image (the stone, the reflection in the glass) is strong, and the restraint of the final scene lands. Below is a categorized punch list of fixable issues — typos through structure.


WHAT'S WORKING

  • The opening breakfast / inhaler / pinky promise exchange establishes Sarah's character entirely through small instructions. Show, don't tell.

  • The window-reflection beat on pages 5–6, where Sarah and James "occupy the same space in the glass," is a strong central image.

  • The tunnel echo on page 9 — voiceover only audible in darkness — is a beautiful restraint choice.

  • The two pockets / two hands ending gives the audience the metaphor without telegraphing it.

GRAMMAR & PUNCTUATION

  • Page 2 — "But what if its really early?" → it's (same fix twice on this page).

  • Page 4 — "I love you too, Mommy." Add comma of direct address; capitalize Mommy.

  • Page 13 — last action line is missing a period: "...like he's still deciding what it means"


CAPITALIZATION

  • Page 2 — JAMES's dialogue: "even on Saturdays?" → "Even on Saturdays?" (dialogue lines start uppercase).

  • Pages 3 and 4 — "beat." appears lowercase, but it's "Beat." everywhere else. Pick one (industry standard is Beat.).

  • Page 4 — "mommy" used as direct address → Mommy.

  • Page 11 — "offering the stone to dan" → Dan.

  • Page 11 — "Something flicks In his mind." Mid-sentence In should be lowercase.


CONTINUITY ERROR

  Eyes opening twice. End of page 9: "James opens his eyes." Then page 11
  opens with: "James opens his eyes, looks down at the stone." — but his
  eyes were already open from page 10's "What is that?" exchange.

  Fix: delete the second "opens his eyes," OR change page 9 to keep his
  eyes closed through Dan's question.


FORMAT / SCREENPLAY CONVENTION

  Page 13 — Broken V.O. block. As laid out, the dialogue is split awkwardly:

      JAMES (V.O.)
      I'll call Mommy tonight. Tell

      her all about the train ride. Beat.

  The orphan line and trailing "Beat." should be:

      JAMES (V.O.)
      I'll call Mommy tonight. Tell her all about the train ride.

      Beat.

  • Page 7 — The spreadsheet description is a dense paragraph of action.
    Cleaner: lead with INSERT - LAPTOP SCREEN and break it into 2–3 short
    visual lines so the reader knows they're meant to read the screen.

  • Page 5 — Three INT. TRAIN - CONTINUOUS headers stack within seconds.
    Some directors love the rhythm; a script reader may flag it. Could
    collapse two into one.

  • Page 12 — "mid type" → "mid-type" (or "mid-typing").


VERIFY WITH THE WRITER (NOT ERRORS)

  • Surname mismatch. James and Dan are SHNIDER; Sarah is ORCHIN.
    Intentional (kept maiden name / already separated)? If yes, fine. If
    no, align them.

  • "SHNIDER" spelling. Unusual vs. Schneider / Snider — stylized on
    purpose?

  • Dan's Bloomington/limestone speech (page 4). Dan says "the ground
    they're standing on" while they're 200 miles away in Kentucky. Reads
    slightly off geographically. Probably intentional (nervous trivia-
    dumping) — but a director may push back.

  • Buried backstory. Lines like "a man who knows exactly what he's done"
    (page 3), "older than this argument" (page 4), the phone photo (page
    9), and the undealt business cards (page 13) all hint at something
    specific — affair, forced career move, betrayal. The ambiguity is a
    craft choice. If the writer wants the audience to land on a specific
    read, one or two of these may need to point harder.
