## Issue 1 — What it Takes to Build Something Exceptional (September 28, 2024)

Hey, it's Sai.

Welcome to *Building in College*, where I share my journey as a freshman in college balancing academics, entrepreneurship, content, and life. If you're seeing this, it means you've impacted my life in some way or the other. So thank you :)

What's in this week's issue:
- Healthy vs Unhealthy Ego (and how i'm trying to minimize the ladder)
- What it takes to build something exceptional
- Lessons from knocking on random dorms

### Healthy vs Unhealthy Ego

After about an hour of getting our asses kicked at Yoga Club (one of the 2 clubs I joined in college), my friends and I had a treacherous walk back to the dorm.

The conversations on the way back to the dorm got really deep (probably a by-product of the yoga). It led to one of my friend asking me "What's the difference between healthy and unhealthy ego?".

As someone who has been trying to work on their ego for the last year, I thought I had the perfect answer. But after some thinking, I couldn't come up with an answer.

The truth? I had no idea what the difference was. But, ironically, I gave him a half-assed answer that I didn't really believe in.

So for the last 2 weeks I've set out on a quest to deepen my understanding of good and bad ego.

Ego, in its most literal definition, is a person's sense of self esteem or self-importance.

Ego, as I see it, is a spectrum. Too little of it leads to less self-love and submissiveness. Too much of it leads to arrogance and entitlement.

As you read this, you can probably think of people in your life on either extreme. And let's be honest, we all wish they had more of a balance.

But at what point is the line drawn? What is the PERFECT balance of ego?

Well, there isn't.

Think about it. If you want to be a billionaire, you would need more of an ego than if you want to be a monk. Is that a bad thing? No. Different goals require different amounts of ego.

There is only one way to find the perfect balance: Reflection.

As corny as it sounds, there is no other way to reduce your bad ego and increase your good. Your life is unique to you. Hence, requiring your own reflection.

My challenge for you this week: *Reflect*

Some questions to reflect on at the end of everyday:

When was the last time I felt proud of myself? Was that pride healthy, or did it veer into arrogance?
Have I been overly critical of myself or others recently? Why?
How do I handle feedback? Do I accept it gracefully, or do I become defensive?
In what situations do I feel my ego gets the best of me, and when does it empower me?

Healthy and Unhealthy Ego isn't as black and white as it seems. It's more complex than that. Only you have the right answer. As I try to reflect more on my ego, I hope you do as well.

### What it takes to build something exceptional

In September of 2023, I was making more money than I knew to do with. I moved to downtown Miami, lived in a high-rise, spent $10k/month+ on my personal expenses, and bought whatever I wanted. I was living my childhood dream.

5 months later, it all came crashing down.

We started our company in 2021, and most of our clients came through referrals. Pretty much every client we worked with saw results. So, we decided to scale. We hired a few people and started signing clients left and right. It was a walk in the park - or so we thought.

As we scaled, so did our problems. We assumed that our team would know how to serve clients the way we did - a stupid assumption. We had spent 0 time building processes and systems prior to bringing on more clients. And so, clients started churning out because they weren't seeing results.

And so, we scaled back down a few months ago and started building our processes, brick by brick.

I'm a big believer in ethical business. If you can't follow through on your promises, then you need to rethink being in business. And if you build something on unethical grounds, it's only a matter of time until karma catches up to you.

It takes a big hit to your ego when you make a lot less money than you used to - a really tough pill to swallow.

But I feel much more fulfilled, knowing I'm doing the right thing, even if it means making less money for now.

To build something exceptional, you need to be able to go to bed peacefully at night, knowing that you tried your absolute best to deliver on the promises you made.

If you can honestly believe this in the depths of your heart, then it's just a matter of time until the universe starts to work in your favor. Building something exceptional is hard. But if you decide to build anything, go the extra mile and make it exceptional. It's worth it.

### Lessons from knocking on random dorms

One of my reasons for going to college was building my personal brand in a unique way.

Making content is something I've always been passionate about. I used to make a lot of content on TikTok. And even though I was racking up hundreds of millions of views a month, it wasn't necessarily true to me.

My philosophy is that a personal brand is a reflection of who you are. And so I've been careful to take my time on creating content that actually resonates with me. Recently, however, I think I've landed on the idea: Learning from strangers.

I see a lot of content on my feed of really rich and successful people giving advice to the younger generation. I love the videos - and the tips are super valuable. But it makes me think of a quote I once heard:

"Learn everything you can, anytime you can, from anyone you can, there will always come a time when you will be grateful you did"

It feels like we've lost touch with valuing the life lessons of everyday people. It seems like we only pay attention to those who are old or wealthy. I also think this is a big reason why there is no unity in the world now. Everyone is too full of themselves to be open-minded to other people's advice/opinions.

And that made me want to create content around this. The idea? Knock on college student's dorms and ask them what their biggest life lesson is.

I'm not sure how well this content will perform, but it's more true to me than the tons of views I used to get :)

### Using Game Theory to Get a Competitive Edge:

Understanding game theory makes me see most situations as part of a bigger game we're all playing.

Game theory (simply put) can applied to anything that has the following components:
- **Players**: The decision-makers involved in the interaction.
- **Strategies**: The possible actions each player can take.
- **Payoffs**: The outcomes or rewards resulting from the combination of players' strategies.
- **Games**: The specific scenarios or models that describe the rules and structure of the interaction.

To sum it up… it's like dinner with extended family. Everyone has an agenda, the stakes feel weirdly high, and no one leaves without a few bruised egos.

Game theory can give you a competitive edge, with proven strategies to outcompete others.

Since most of my day is centered around business, I try to use game theory as much as I can.

In my quest to use game theory to gain a competitive edge in business, I stumbled upon a story from Alex Hormozi that profoundly changed how I approach things.

It goes something like this…

*Imagine: You and a friend play a dice game. You are each given one die. One of the die has 20 sides. The other has 200. On each die, only one side is green. And the rest are red. The goal is simple: roll green as many times as possible.*

*The rules are as follows:*
- *You can't see how many sides you have. You can only see if you roll red or green.*
- *If you roll green – One of your red sides turns green, and you get to roll again.*
- *If you roll red – Nothing happens, and you get to roll again.*
- *The game ends when you stop rolling; if you stop, you lose.*

*You keep rolling persistently.*

*At first, greens are rare, but as more red sides turn green, greens come up more often. Eventually, rolling green becomes common.*

*Your friend rolls a few times, gets red each time, and sees you roll green. He complains that you must have the easier die. He spends more time watching and grumbling than playing. Frustrated by how long it takes, he quits.*

*So who got the die with 20 sides? Who got the die with 200 sides? If you get the game then you see, once you roll enough times, the die you're given doesn't matter.*

*Die with fewer sides might roll green sooner.*
*Die with more sides might roll green later.*
*But, a die with a green side always has a chance of rolling green… if you roll it.*

*All of us get a many-sided die. And looking at the other players, you have no idea if it's their 100th roll or their 100,000th. You don't know how "good" other players are when they start, you can only see how well they do now. But, if you understand the game, you also know it doesn't matter.*

*A few begin playing early. Others begin much later. The rest sit on the sidelines complaining about how lucky the players are. I guess so, but they're luckier because they play. And when they hit red, which they do, guess what they do? They roll again.*

I discovered that the competitive edge I was looking for was much simpler than I thought. There are no life hacks or secret shortcuts. It's all about persistently playing the game - rolling the dice repeatedly without giving up.

### Should You Think About Death?

My dad turned 62 the other day. I don't like to remind him of his age, but I'm always curious how he views life at 62. The average life expectancy for men in America is 79. What is it like knowing this at the age of 62?

So I said screw it, and built up the courage to ask him. I asked "Are you afraid of death". At first he said no. Then my mom (who overheard my question) told him to be honest. He then responded with "I try not to think about it".

I somehow expected this answer. Most people "try not to think about it" because they can't do anything about it. Which makes sense, in theory. After all, nobody likes a reminder that their warranty is, well, non-renewable.

But I took the last few days to reflect on his answer. Should I be thinking about death, or simply ignore it?

But the more I thought about it, the more I realized that fully ignoring it might not be the smartest thing to do.

It's like being a farmer during the harvest season. You know the growing season is limited, and that winter will inevitably come. You could avoid thinking about it, but that might mean you're less prepared for the change in seasons. By accepting that winter is coming, you're reminded to gather your crops, appreciate the harvest, and prepare for the colder days ahead.

Now there is obviously the other extreme to this: thinking about death too much. That's when you start to get that weird feeling in your stomach, not fun 😂

The balance is somewhere in between - just enough awareness to make sure you're living fully in each season, savoring the warmth while it lasts, and readying yourself for whatever comes next.

## Issue 3 — Advice from CEO of the Hawks & Applying it to Trendify (October 20, 2024)

The best part about college is getting to learn from incredible guest speakers.

I was recently able to meet the CEO of the Hawks, and the founder of a multi 8 figure agency.

And the best part about you being on my newsletter? You get to read these lessons - without having to pay $9,790 a year in tuition.

My goal for each of the 2 interactions was simple… learn as much as possible, and get them both on my newsletter. Done and done. Now I just have to figure out how to keep them from unsubscribing 🤣

Here are my biggest takeaways from how they operate (and later, I'll share some unique ways I'm applying these lessons to my own business).

- Stick to one thing for an extended period of time. Master your craft - seriously master it.
- People are everything. Find the right people to grow your business.
- Provide tons of value to people you admire, with no expectation of return.
- Your customers ARE your business, so create amazing experiences for them (unique tips on this later)
- Your product should market itself before you actively market it.
- Go above and beyond in everything.

### Applying Lessons:

I've been reading *Siddhartha* (Buddha's story), which follows a young boy on his path to enlightenment.

The part that stood out to me was when Siddhartha meets Gotama, the Buddha himself. After hearing Gotama speak, Siddhartha realizes something profound. He doesn't need to learn more through teachings. Instead, he needs to experience life for himself. It got me thinking about the difference between learning and experiencing.

While learning from successful people is incredible, real growth happens through experience and action. Siddhartha realized he needed to experience life for himself beyond teachings. I've learned from this, that no amount of knowledge will matter unless you apply it. Sounds simple, seems obvious - but really think about it. How many things have you "learned" but never applied?

Alex Hormozi defines learning as "Same conditions, different behavior." Are you changing your behavior when facing the same conditions? If not, you haven't truly learned.

### How I'm Changing my Behavior:

The most important takeaway from both conversations was the idea of going above and beyond. Every lesson they shared can be distilled into that simple, yet powerful principle.

So, how could I apply this to everything in my business?

Here's what we're currently implementing at my company Trendify. As a refresher, *Trendify* helps companies scale through short-form content.

I've organized it into three buckets: Customers, Marketing, and Internal Team.

**Customers:**
- For every 5 videos we create, we donate 1 to the client's nonprofit of choice. A cool way to create impact.
- Custom care packages for new clients with merch, handwritten cards, and QR codes that link to personalized intro videos.
- 10 free static image ads for every client.
- Significant investments into R&D and data systems for more predictable outcomes for clients.

**Marketing:**
- Finding 100 companies we can truly help and delivering value to them in every way possible.
  - Free, custom video ad creative sent to them (video we sent to Indeed).
  - Custom care packages with Trendify gear and handwritten notes.
  - Personalized emails delivering real value.
- Custom Trendify-branded care packages sent to VC firms with our ideal portfolio companies.

**Internal Team:**
- Team bonding activities (retreats, dinners, etc.).
- Fun incentives to make more $: New hires share two truths and one lie. The team guesses which is which, and correct answers earn a gift card. It's a fun way to break the ice and help new hires feel welcome. (Shamelessly stole this from Warby Parker.)
- During the hiring process, we ask what their $10 guilty pleasure is. We send it via mail along with a handwritten note as soon as they're hired.

The longer I've been building Trendify, the more I realize that most companies are focused on making money. Which is cool - no judgment at all.

But I have a hypothesis about the companies that will succeed over the next 50 years. They won't optimize for money; they'll optimize for creating great experiences in every area of their business. And as a result, they'll make more money than most.

We'll see how this holds up in 50 years :)

My advice to the business owners reading this: Break down every part of your customer experience and make it unique - go above and beyond. It's such a fun exercise and yields outsized returns.

My question to you: What have you learned from this? And how are you going to apply it? (respond to this email and let me know).

### Charlie Kirk's Political Strategies

I recently found out that *Charlie Kirk* was coming to my school to debate students on politics. **As an independent, I was curious to watch it all go down.**

And so I went.

The atmosphere was interesting. Loud "USA". chants within the first few minutes, followed by "Trump" chants.

Hundreds of MAGA hats were tossed into the crowd before they allowed anyone to step up and debate Charlie.

Watching it all, **I found myself analyzing the strategy behind each move**, paying close attention to what he and his team were doing.

And, honestly, the simple act of tossing MAGA hats struck me as genius. Here's why:

1. Charlie only threw a few hats at a time into the crowd. Everyone was jumping to catch one - making the hats seem super exclusive.
2. Each video Charlie Kirk posted from the event shows students proudly wearing MAGA hats, leveraging social proof to sway undecided viewers.
3. After the event, those hats were worn ALL over campus, spreading Trump's message to people who didn't even attend.

The most interesting part is that **Charlie never wears a hat himself** - not at this event or any others. This makes him appear less overly pro-Trump, which probably helps him connect with undecided voters that watch his content.

It's cool experiencing in person what you usually only see on your feed. Some of these moves are so well orchestrated, it's mind-blowing.

For instance, way behind this massive crowd - easily half a mile away - someone on Charlie Kirk's team called for anyone who disagreed to be escorted straight to the mic. Charlie's team was actively seeking out disagreeing voices for debate, because, 9 times out of 10, those exchanges go viral on social media.

It's genius.

But it also raises questions about how social media subtly 'micro-brainwashes' us, using simple psychology to shape perspectives. Some might call it manipulation; others see it as a carefully crafted strategy.

### Ethical Business

A few months ago, I was having a conversation with a friend that went something like this:

Friend: "What do you think of billionaires? "
Me: "Some of them seem like douches, others seem cool"
Friend: "All Billionaires are unethical. You know they exploit people to make that much money?"
Me: *Sort of visibly angry trying best to compose myself* - "I disagree"

I hadn't thought about that conversation until we recently scaled ***down*** our business to make customer fulfillment truly exceptional.

It wasn't unethical by any means, but we knew it could be much better - and that was worth the trade-off. We knew we had to build something way better to scale it into something great.

Now I want you to think of people who scale companies with unethical products/services. Guess what? There's no way in hell they are becoming billionaires.

I'll take it a step further.

There are people with great products/services. But, they treat their employees like shit. Guess what? There's no way in hell they are becoming billionaires either.

Think of companies worth billions. You've probably used their products, and chances are, someone you know works there too. They make great products, and their employee experience isn't "exploitative".

Becoming a billionaire isn't as easy as it looks. If you spent all your energy trying to exploit people, you'd be further from a billion than if you focused on building something ethical and valuable.

Now, obviously, there are anomalies - people who cut corners or mistreat people and still make it big. But they're the exceptions, not the standard.

Spoiler alert: I'm not a billionaire 🤣 but after years of building, scaling down, and figuring out what actually works, here's what I've picked up while watching the billionaire club from deep in the nosebleeds.

I'm a big believer that the more ethical you are, the more money you will make.

## Issue 6 — Fear: the most useless emotion (February 12, 2025)

*Why fear in most modern day scenarios is a really useless emotion*

Hey, it's Sai Karra, checking in from a local Starbucks. I know you missed me ;)

Welcome to my newsletter, where I *used to* dive into the chaos of building a business in college.

I dropped out a few months ago. So I guess now it's just the chaos of building a business *without a safety net* (stakes are higher than ever).

What's in this week's issue:
- Fear

### Fear

Alright, this newsletter is mainly for myself. I thought about journaling instead, but this felt more practical.

I visited my old college last week and asked a bunch of my friends what they were afraid of in life.

This sounds lame to admit, but the reason I asked was so they'd ask me back - giving me a chance to open up and have them tell me why my fears were irrelevant (which I needed to hear).

I told them: "I fear that something will go wrong when we scale our business, which is getting in my way of scaling it"

They shit on it (in a nice way). And it helped. But not fully.

As i've been trying to conquer every last bit of this fear, I've been asking myself "what am I actually scared of?"

And here's the truth.

I've spent the *last year of my life* building my business with my brother, into something I was truly proud of.

And now when the time has come to aggressively market, i'm scared.

Scared that something will go wrong
Scared that a client will be upset with something
Scared that an employee will fuck up
Scared that my reputation will be damaged
Scared that something out of my control will go wrong

But it's weird. Because I am genuinely proud of what I built, and think it's incredibly valuable.

Yet, I still haven't pulled the trigger on marketing. Because of the fear that's been holding me down from doing so.

So what now?

I am going to scale, ***despite my fear.***

I realized that fear is literally a signal from our brain bracing us for survival. That's why we feel fear.

But if your fear has nothing to do with your survival, then it's literally the most useless emotion ever.

Think about it…

If you're not going to die, then what the hell are you afraid of?

Most of your fears are ***irrelevant*** to your survival.

Your ancestors didn't beat diseases and fight wars for you to be scared of "damaging your reputation". So don't let them down by being a *bitch*.

Bravery isn't about being fearless - it's about taking action despite being afraid.

I am choosing to be brave. And I hope you do *too*.

## Issue 7 — I lost sleep over AI, until I realized this... (May 31, 2025)

*How I plan on thriving in the AI world, regardless of what happens*

Alright, here's my once in a blue-moon newsletter appearance.

> If you're new here, welcome to my newsletter. My name is Sai Karra. And in this newsletter, I dive into the chaos of building a business as a 20 year old - and share a few (somewhat unrelated) things I'm passionate about along the way.

I don't care where you're at in life — I think this will be worth the read.

I've been holding off on writing this until I actually felt like I had something real to say.

So if you've got 60 seconds, read this through.

### How to create something that thrives in the AI chaos

I've been losing sleep over AI.

Like, seriously.

I've gone down multiple existential spirals — from the possibility of AI killing my business, to AI creating a full-blown dystopia.

As someone who wants to achieve worldly success, AI genuinely scares me. And I think I'd be ignorant if it didn't. It's getting too good, too fast.

But weirdly, I'm glad it shook me.

Because it forced me to start thinking in a completely different way.

To ask, **"If everything changes… how do I still win?"**

From a business perspective, I used to just shrug and think, "Whatever happens, happens. I'm not gonna build the next ChatGPT. And even if I did, OpenAI or Google would build something better and wipe me out."

Something I realized about myself is that I think in extremes by default.

Not something I chose — just how my brain works.

So when I think about making our business "AI-proof," my mind jumps to a post-apocalyptic world where everything I care about is gone because of AI.

Every time that dystopian thought hits, I ask:

**"How would I still thrive in *that* world?"**

And then… I take that answer and apply it right back to the business.

### So what's the answer?

Most entrepreneurs think surviving AI means building AI or using it smarter.

But for *most people*, I think the key is actually the opposite.

While everyone flocks to building the next AI model, I think they'll overlook what gets more valuable as a result.

And that's what gives me hope.

**Because for every action, there's an equal and opposite reaction.**

Not just in physics — but in how humans work.

The more we automate, the more we crave what can't be automated.
The more reality gets blurred, the more we chase what feels *undeniably human*.

AI might flood the world with content, but people will still pay a premium for what's *real*. For clarity. For connection. For meaning.

**Status won't die. Community won't disappear.**

Since the beginning of time, we've wanted to stand out — and belong.

Those instincts don't fade. If anything, they'll get louder.

So whether society collapses or becomes a perfect utopia, these core human principles won't change. And history doesn't lie — we've had versions of both. These principles were always there.

While AI keeps evolving like crazy, my goal is to go even deeper into the *human* stuff. To study the instincts that get amplified, not replaced by technology. And to build every brand, business, and piece of content around those instincts.

### Some AI-proof principles I recommend applying consistently to your brand or business:

**1. Trust**
When everything starts to feel fake, real becomes gold. People won't care how smart your brand sounds — they'll care if they believe you.

**2. Connection**
AI can talk like us. But it can't *feel* like us. Nothing beats being truly seen, heard, and felt by another human.

**3. Status & Differentiation**
We've always wanted to stand out. Used to be war paint and spears. Now it's followers and flexes. That won't change.

**4. Meaning & Story**
People don't just want to buy things — they want a *reason to care*. A story they can see themselves in. That's where real loyalty starts. Building a real brand is huge here.

**5. Beauty & Emotion**
AI can optimize for clicks. But it can't move you. Rawness, beauty, and real emotion still hit harder than logic.

It's up to you how you apply all this - but here's what I've been doing every week:

I write down the biggest advancements in AI I've seen. Then I go department by department in our business and ask:

**"What needs to change because of this?"**

Like when Google dropped *Veo 3*, I asked:

**"How do we make our marketing feel *even more* real now that fake content's about to get scary good?"**

I jot the answers down. Then we actually start testing them.

Yeah, you *could* argue that even these principles might get replaced by AI someday. But even if that happens, it'll take a long, long time.

And even then… I don't think humans will fully accept it.

**Real world example of how I'm using these things in my business…**

We started hyper personalizing EVERYTHING we send out to prospects. We now spend actual time and tons of money on ultra-personalized marketing material.
