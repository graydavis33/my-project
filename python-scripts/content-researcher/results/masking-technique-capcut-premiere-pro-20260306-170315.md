## TOP 5 HOOK PATTERNS

**Hook Pattern 1: The Outcome-First Title**
Name what the viewer will be able to DO, not what the video IS. Frame it as a visual result, not a process.
*Why it works:* Viewers are searching for outcomes, not education. When your title is the answer to their search query, click-through skyrockets because there's zero ambiguity about value.
*Example:* "Text Behind You in CapCut Tutorial (New & Easy Way)" — the hook is the outcome ("text behind you"), not the method.

**Hook Pattern 2: The Superlative Shortcut**
Lead with "BEST," "FASTEST," or "EASIEST" to signal that the viewer is getting a curated recommendation, not raw information.
*Why it works:* It collapses decision fatigue. The viewer doesn't need to watch five videos and compare — this one has already done the filtering. It's a trust transfer.
*Example:* "The BEST EFFECTS to use in CAPCUT!" — the all-caps superlative promises editorial judgment the viewer doesn't have to do themselves.

**Hook Pattern 3: The Progression Promise**
Signal that the video moves the viewer from a lower state to a higher state — beginner to competent, unknown to known.
*Why it works:* It addresses two audiences at once (beginners who need it and intermediates who fear they're missing something), and it frames the content as transformation rather than information.
*Example:* "Master Masking in CapCut Step-by-Step Editing Tutorial CapCut Explained Beginner to Pro Guide" — "Beginner to Pro" is explicit transformation language.

**Hook Pattern 4: The Automation/Efficiency Signal**
Lead with a word that implies the technique saves time or removes effort ("Auto," "Fast," "Easy," "One Click").
*Why it works:* Editors are perpetually time-constrained. Anything that signals "you don't have to do this the hard way" triggers an immediate emotional response — relief.
*Example:* "Auto Track MASK | Premiere Pro" — the word "Auto" is the entire hook. It implies the software does the work, not the editor.

**Hook Pattern 5: The Visual Impossibility**
Describe an effect that sounds like it shouldn't be possible with simple tools.
*Why it works:* It creates a curiosity gap — the viewer can picture the result but can't immediately explain how it's done, which makes watching feel like solving a mystery.
*Example:* "Video inside Text in Premiere Pro Tutorial" — "video inside text" sounds like it requires advanced skills, but the 25-second runtime implies it's actually simple, creating an irresistible tension.
